This paper sits in the design-based causal-inference tradition that begins with randomized experiments and potential outcomes. Fisher’s randomization logic, Rubin’s potential-outcomes formulation, and the Horvitz--Thompson inverse-probability estimator provide the basic estimand-and-estimator language for finite populations. The interference problem relaxes SUTVA by allowing potential outcomes to depend on the full assignment vector; Hudgens and Halloran, Aronow and Samii, Athey--Eckles--Imbens, Sävje--Aronow--Hudgens, Leung, Li--Wager, and the recent Bhadra--Schweinberger review develop the main inference framework for such settings. Manski’s work on social interactions and homophily clarifies why network structure and similar-unit sorting are not nuisance details but central objects.

The closest experimental-design lineage studies how to choose assignments when interference is transmitted through a network. Ugander et al. introduced graph-cluster randomization for network exposure, Eckles--Karrer--Ugander studied bias reduction through correlated assignment, Baird et al. considered optimal experiment design under interference, and Ugander--Yin randomized the clustering itself. More recent work refines the design frontier: Brennan--Mirrokni--Pouget-Abadie handle one-sided bipartite experiments; Cortez-Rodriguez--Eichhorn--Yu analyze clustered rollout designs; Fatemi--Zheleva emphasize the tension between reducing interference and avoiding selection imbalance; Viviano et al. formulate causal clustering through an SDP; Chen et al. directly optimize treatment-assignment covariance; and Cai--Zhang--Airoldi propose independent-set designs for estimating treatment and spillover effects. The anchor paper, Thiyageswaran et al., is the immediate predecessor because it makes the design covariance \(X=\mathbb{E}[xx^\top]\) the decision variable and optimizes a worst-case-MSE SDP trading off interference, homophily, and robustness.

The second methodological strand is covariate balancing and discrepancy-based experimental design. Efron, Morgan--Rubin, Kallus, and Harshaw--Sävje--Spielman--Zhang formalize assignment mechanisms that trade randomization robustness against balance over observed or constructed covariates. Bansal and Lovett--Meka provide the algorithmic discrepancy background behind walk-based balancing methods, while the anchor paper adapts this perspective to graph-Laplacian embeddings induced by interference and homophily. Relative to these works, the proposed contribution is not another estimator for a fixed assignment rule; it asks when the SDP optimum itself is implementable by an actual randomized \(\{\pm1\}\) design and how large the implementability loss is when exactness fails.

The optimization and graph-theoretic side is equally central. Goemans--Williamson establish the canonical SDP relaxation and Gaussian rounding template for MAXCUT, while Deza--Laurent and later elliptope work such as Bandeira--Kunisky separate the elliptope relaxation from the cut/correlation polytope of realizable \(\{\pm1\}\) second moments. Boyd--Vandenberghe provide the convex-optimization background. For the graph families named in the proposal, Lazarsfeld--Merton, McPherson--Smith-Lovin--Cook, Holland--Laskey--Leinhardt, Newman, Jackson, Abbe, and Banerjee--Chandrasekhar--Duflo--Jackson supply the homophily, assortative-network, SBM, and empirical-network context. The paper’s positioning is therefore a bridge: it recasts the cut-versus-spread transition in interference-aware experimental design as an exactness and rounding-loss phase structure for implementable \(\{\pm1\}\) design covariances.
