Difference-in-differences is rooted in program-evaluation designs that use repeated observations to construct untreated counterfactual trends, with early labor-market applications such as Ashenfelter and Card (1985) and Card and Krueger (1994) shaping the empirical template. Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) made inference in serially correlated DiD designs a central concern, while Abadie (2005), Athey and Imbens (2006), Imbens and Wooldridge (2009), and Angrist and Pischke (2009) provide the canonical semiparametric, nonlinear, and potential-outcome foundations. The proposed paper belongs to this lineage but works on the multiplicative mean scale: the untreated outcome restriction is Wooldridge's (2023) exponential/Poisson parallel-trends formulation rather than the additive untreated-outcome trend used in linear DiD.

The closest methodological antecedent is the modern staggered-adoption literature showing that naive two-way fixed-effects regressions need not estimate an interpretable average treatment effect under heterogeneous effects. Goodman-Bacon (2021) decomposes the linear TWFE coefficient into two-by-two DiD comparisons, while de Chaisemartin and D'Haultfoeuille (2020) show that linear TWFE can place negative weights on group-time treatment effects. Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021), Sun and Abraham (2021), Borusyak, Jaravel, and Spiess (2024), Gardner (2022), and Baker et al. (2025) develop interpretable group-time, event-study, imputation, and practitioner-facing alternatives. The present result is positioned as the Poisson/multiplicative analogue of the forbidden-comparison problem: it characterizes the PPML pseudo-true coefficient through a misspecified score rather than a linear weighted-sum decomposition.

The Poisson side builds on pseudo-maximum likelihood and count-data econometrics. White (1982), Andrews (1988), and Gourieroux, Monfort, and Trognon (1984a,b) give the misspecified likelihood and pseudo-likelihood asymptotic foundation; Hausman, Hall, and Griliches (1984), McCullagh and Nelder (1989), Cameron and Trivedi (2013), and Correia, Guimaraes, and Zylkin (2020) are the natural references for fixed-effect Poisson/count-data estimation and high-dimensional PPML computation. Santos Silva and Tenreyro (2006, 2011) explain why PPML is central for multiplicative mean models, especially in gravity applications with zeros and heteroskedasticity. The proposed theorem uses this apparatus to study the probability limit of a misspecified fixed-effect Poisson single-coefficient regression, not to justify PPML as correctly specified.

The directly related frontier is Moreau-Kastler (2025), which formulates proportional treatment effects in staggered PPML settings and motivates positive counterfactual-share-weighted proportional ATT targets, and Wooldridge (2023), which gives simple nonlinear DiD identification and estimation under exponential mean parallel trends. Roth and Sant'Anna (2023) are relevant because they clarify how DiD conclusions can depend on outcome transformations and functional form. In applied trade settings, Anderson and van Wincoop (2003), Head and Mayer (2014), Yotov et al. (2016), Santos Silva and Tenreyro (2006), and Nagengast and Yotov (2025) explain why multiplicative gravity models, PPML, and staggered treatment timing meet in practice. The contribution therefore sits at the intersection of staggered-DiD heterogeneity diagnostics and nonlinear PPML estimation: it does not replace the positive-weight PTT target, but explains why the naive FE-PPML coefficient can move in the wrong direction when the pseudo-true weighted-FWL residual of treatment is negative for already-treated comparison cells.
