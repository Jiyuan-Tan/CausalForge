\section{Appendix A. Algebra, Testing, and Witness-Law Details}

This appendix collects the algebraic details behind the lower-bound construction in \cref{obj:thm:minimax-lower}.  The purpose is only to connect the calibration, membership, divergence, separation, and testing ingredients used by \cref{obj:thm:minimax-lower}.  Throughout, the rate statement is a lower-bound statement over the observed-law class in \cref{obj:def:law-class}.

The exponent calculation begins with the overlap envelope in \cref{obj:prop:overlap-envelope}.  For a local contrast scale \(h\), an active block of mass proportional to \(h^\alpha\), and weak-arm probability proportional to \(h^\beta\), the overlap-decay condition can be tested on the tight admissible window
\[
v=h^\beta,
\qquad
u=h^{\beta/\gamma}.
\]
On that window,
\[
u^\alpha v^{1/\gamma}
=
h^{\alpha\beta/\gamma}h^{\beta/\gamma}
=
h^{(\alpha+1)\beta/\gamma}.
\]
Since \(0<h<1\), the envelope \(u^\alpha v^{1/\gamma}\) is at least of order \(h^\alpha\) precisely when
\[
\frac{(\alpha+1)\beta}{\gamma}\le \alpha,
\qquad\text{or equivalently}\qquad
\beta\le \frac{\alpha\gamma}{\alpha+1}.
\]
This is the calibration recorded in \cref{obj:prop:overlap-envelope}.  It identifies the weakest admissible treatment-arm probability on the active block, and therefore explains why the denominator in \cref{obj:def:exponents} contains the additional term \(\beta_{\alpha,\gamma}\) when \(\gamma>0\).

The witness laws in \cref{obj:def:two-point-witness} are designed so that this calibration is binding.  The covariate law is Lebesgue measure on \([0,1]\), the active block has mass \(P_X(B_n)=c_Bh_n^\alpha\), and the logging propensity on that block is \(q_n\), equal to \(1/4\) in the strict-overlap endpoint and to \(h_n^{\beta_{\alpha,\gamma}}\) in the weak-overlap case.  Off the block, both laws have the same strictly positive contrast \(\tau_0\).  On the block, they differ only by the sign of the treated-arm conditional mean, so that the two oracle rules are \(x\mapsto 1\) and \(x\mapsto \mathbf 1\{x\notin B_n\}\), as stated in \cref{obj:lem:witness-membership}.  The membership lemma is also where the availability of these two rules in the policy class is recorded for the two-action reduction.

The chi-square calculation in \cref{obj:lem:two-point-divergence} has only one contributing cell.  The laws coincide outside \(B_n\), and they also coincide on the untreated part of \(B_n\).  Conditional on \(X\in B_n\) and \(A=1\), the two outcome distributions are Bernoulli laws on \(\{-1,1\}\) with success probabilities \((1+h_n)/2\) and \((1-h_n)/2\).  Their one-cell chi-square divergence is of order \(h_n^2\).  Multiplying by the probability of entering the cell gives
\[
\chi^2(P_{n,+}\,\|\,P_{n,-})
\lesssim
P_X(B_n)q_nh_n^2
\asymp
h_n^{\alpha+\beta_{\alpha,\gamma}+2}.
\]
With
\[
h_n=n^{-1/(2+\alpha+\beta_{\alpha,\gamma})},
\]
the product divergence remains bounded because
\[
n h_n^{2+\alpha+\beta_{\alpha,\gamma}}\asymp 1.
\]
Equivalently, the product formula
\[
1+\chi^2(P_{n,+}^{\otimes n}\,\|\,P_{n,-}^{\otimes n})
=
\left(1+\chi^2(P_{n,+}\,\|\,P_{n,-})\right)^n
\]
is bounded by a constant, as in \cref{obj:lem:two-point-divergence}.

The regret calculation in \cref{obj:lem:regret-separation} uses the fact that the two oracle assignments disagree exactly on \(B_n\).  Any single deterministic policy must assign at least half of the block incorrectly for one of the two laws, up to the constant convention in the lemma.  On that law, the welfare identity in \cref{obj:thm:welfare-identity} gives a pointwise regret weight \(h_n\) on the misclassified part of \(B_n\).  Thus the loss is bounded below by a constant multiple of
\[
P_X(B_n)h_n
\asymp
h_n^{1+\alpha}.
\]
Substituting the calibrated bandwidth gives
\[
h_n^{1+\alpha}
=
n^{-(1+\alpha)/(2+\alpha+\beta_{\alpha,\gamma})}
=
n^{-r_\star(\alpha,\gamma)}.
\]

The final step is the two-point testing reduction.  \Cref{obj:lem:le-cam-two-point-chisq} is the chi-square version of Le Cam's method \citep{LeCam1986,Tsybakov2009}: bounded product divergence prevents every test from identifying the sign with vanishing total error.  Applying that testing bound to the two witness laws converts the regret separation into the minimax lower bound in \cref{obj:thm:minimax-lower}.  Its exponent characterizes the converse construction, while feasible policy-learning upper rates are developed through a separate achievability analysis.
