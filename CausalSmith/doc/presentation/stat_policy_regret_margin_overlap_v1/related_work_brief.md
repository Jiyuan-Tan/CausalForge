This paper belongs to the statistical treatment-choice and causal policy-learning literature that starts from potential outcomes and propensity-score identification \citep{Neyman1990,Rubin1974,Rosenbaum1983,Imbens2015}. Its welfare-regret estimand follows the decision-theoretic treatment-rule program of \citet{Manski2004,Manski2009} and finite-sample/minimax treatment-choice work such as \citet{Stoye2009}, while its estimators use the semiparametric/AIPW lineage developed for missing data and treatment-effect estimation \citep{Robins1994,Hahn1998,Hirano2003,Newey1994,Bickel1993,VanDerLaan2011,Chernozhukov2018,Chernozhukov2022}. The target here is not a new identification result: under strict overlap the welfare-regret risk functional is point identified. The contribution is an estimation-rate characterization when overlap deteriorates one-sidedly near the propensity boundary.

Within policy learning, \citet{Kitagawa2018} established empirical welfare maximization as a central method for regret control over structured policy classes, and \citet{Athey2021} connected observational-data policy learning to doubly robust scores, giving $n^{-1/2}$-type regret guarantees under regular overlap conditions. Related statistical and machine-learning strands include individualized treatment-rule and weighted-classification methods \citep{Qian2011,Zhao2012,Zhang2012}, doubly robust and counterfactual-risk methods for logged bandit data \citep{Dudik2011,Swaminathan2015}, balanced policy learning \citep{Kallus2017}, and CATE-first approaches that can feed plug-in treatment rules \citep{Nie2021}. Extensions of empirical welfare maximization and policy choice to instrumental-variable, constrained, and time-series settings further show the breadth of the treatment-choice framework \citep{Sasaki2020,Sun2021,Kitagawa2022}.

The rate mechanism in this paper is closest to the margin/low-noise classification literature. \citet{Audibert2007} and \citet{Massart2006} show how Tsybakov-type margin conditions convert estimation error near the decision boundary into faster excess-risk rates, and \citet{Tsybakov2009} and \citet{LeCam1986} provide the nonparametric minimax and testing tools underlying matching lower bounds. \citet{Luedtke2017} is the nearest strict-positivity policy-learning antecedent: it derives faster policy-regret rates by exploiting margin structure, with positivity entering through bounded influence functions. The present result differs by letting the AIPW score itself become ill behaved through one-sided polynomial overlap decay, and by deriving the phase boundary between the margin-driven and overlap-limited regimes from an explicit local Hellinger/KL calibration rather than imposing it externally.

The directly related frontier on overlap and adaptivity is adjacent but not identical. Work on overlap weights, high-dimensional overlap, poor-overlap estimands, and heavy-tailed IPW estimation documents why near-positivity changes variance and target-population behavior \citep{Li2016,DAmour2017,BenMichael2022,Hill2024,Susmann2025}. In policy learning, \citet{Zhao2023} studies positivity-free stochastic policies based on incremental propensity-score interventions, while \citet{Liu2026} studies weight clipping for offline policy learning with upper-bound analysis. Adaptive-data and online policy-learning papers such as \citet{Zhan2021} and \citet{Girard2025} address dependent samples, diminishing exploration, or cumulative/excess-risk guarantees, and \citet{Chen2020} and \citet{Sakaguchi2024} develop related doubly robust off-policy/dynamic-regime guarantees. The present paper is positioned as fixed offline policy learning under one-sided overlap decay, with a clipped/trimmed AIPW ERM upper bound and a matching Le Cam/many-hypothesis lower bound whose core object is the local overlap-margin calibration lemma and the resulting closed-form exponent $r(\alpha,\gamma)$.
