\section{Setup and Assumptions}

We work with a \leanref{S-1}{finite-population monotone-Bernoulli rollout design}. The population consists of a finite unit set \leanref{sym:U_n}{$U_n$} with \(n\) units. The experimenter chooses a sequence of rollout measurement rounds indexed by \(j=0,\ldots,k\), and the contemporaneous assignment vector at round \(j\) is \leanref{sym:Z_j}{$Z_j$}. Let \(\Omega\) denote the finite assignment space generated by the rollout randomization, and let \(\pi\) be the probability mass function on \(\Omega\). For any design-measurable random variable \(X\), \(\mathbb E_\pi X\), \(\operatorname{Var}_\pi(X)\), and \(\operatorname{Cov}_\pi(X_1,X_2)\) denote the corresponding finite-sum expectation, variance, and covariance under \(\pi\).

The potential-outcome notation is finite-population notation: \(Y_i(Z_j)\) is unit \(i\)'s outcome under the assignment vector realized at measurement \(j\), and \leanref{sym:bar_Y_j}{$\bar Y_j$} is the observed finite-population round mean. The static rollout condition fixes the interpretation of these objects.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:static-rollout-consistency}[Static Rollout Consistency]
At each rollout measurement \(j\), the observed round mean is
\[
\bar Y_j
=
n^{-1}\sum_{i\in U_n} Y_i(Z_j).
\]
The potential outcomes \(Y_i(Z_j)\) depend on the contemporaneous assignment vector \(Z_j\), but not on earlier rollout steps.
\end{assumptionv}

\cref{obj:ass:static-rollout-consistency} is the static rollout potential-outcomes condition. It permits arbitrary contemporaneous interference through the assignment vector and indexes each potential outcome by the current assignment, as in static rollout formulations for interference designs \citep{CortezRodriguezEichhornYu2024}.

A schedule is an ordered vector \leanref{sym:p}{$p$} of treated fractions. The rollout budget \leanref{sym:q}{$q$} is the final treated fraction, so the design observes the response curve on the truncated interval \([0,q]\), while the target contrast compares \(u=0\) with \(u=1\).

\begin{definitionv}{def:budgeted-schedule-class}[Budgeted schedule class \(S_{k,q}\)]
The budgeted schedule class is
\[
S_{k,q}
=
\left\{
p\in[0,1]^{k+1}:
p_0=0
\ \text{and}\
0<p_1<\cdots<p_k=q
\right\}.
\]
\end{definitionv}

\cref{obj:def:budgeted-schedule-class} makes the design variable explicit. The econometrician controls the placement of the rollout measurement fractions between the untreated baseline and the budget \(q\); the strict inequalities guarantee distinct interior interpolation nodes for the polynomial problem below.

The mean response curve under a law \(P\) is \leanref{sym:m_P}{$m_P(u)$}. The low-order interference restriction used here is that the design mean along the rollout path is a polynomial of degree at most \(\beta\).

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:beta-order-polynomial}[Polynomial Rollout Mean]
For every law \(P\), the rollout mean curve satisfies
\[
m_P(u)=\sum_{\ell=0}^{\beta} a_{P,\ell}u^\ell
\]
for all \(u\in[0,1]\).
\end{assumptionv}

\cref{obj:ass:beta-order-polynomial} is the beta-order rollout polynomial identity condition. It summarizes low-order interference along the treated-fraction path by requiring the average response to be exhausted by powers \(u^0,\ldots,u^\beta\), a restriction used in polynomial exposure and rollout representations of interference \citep{cortez2022}. The endpoint contrast studied below is \(\tau_P=m_P(1)-m_P(0)\).

The next restriction is a sharp variance envelope that isolates the effect of treated-fraction placement from additional covariance structure.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:round-mean-variance-envelope}[Round-Mean Variance Envelope]
For every law \(P\), every admissible schedule \(p\), and every rollout measurement \(j\),
\[
\operatorname{Var}_\pi(\bar Y_j)\le \frac{\sigma_0^2}{n}.
\]
\end{assumptionv}

\cref{obj:ass:round-mean-variance-envelope} is specific to this analysis. It bounds each round mean by the common scale \leanref{sym:sigma_0^2}{$\sigma_0^2$}/\(n\) while leaving the cross-round covariance signs and magnitudes otherwise unrestricted subject to positive semidefiniteness. This is the envelope under which the worst-case variance reduces to an amplification problem for the estimator weights.

For the low-budget results, the budget remains uniformly below full rollout.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:low-budget-cap}[Low-Budget Cap]
The rollout budget and its cap satisfy
\[
0<q_{\max}\quad\text{and}\quad q\le q_{\max}<1.
\]
\end{assumptionv}

\cref{obj:ass:low-budget-cap} is also specific to this analysis. The cap \leanref{sym:q_max}{$q_{\max}$} keeps the problem in the extrapolation regime: the target endpoint \(u=1\) lies outside the observed interval \([0,q]\). The boundary case \(q=1\) is therefore separated from the low-budget rate calculations.

The admissible law class collects the preceding restrictions and pins the design expectation of each round mean to the polynomial curve at the corresponding schedule point.

\begin{definitionv}{def:rollout-law-class}[Rollout law class \(\mathcal P_\beta\)]
The rollout law class is
\[
\mathcal P_\beta
=
\left\{
P:
\begin{array}{l}
P \text{ satisfies \cref{obj:ass:static-rollout-consistency}, \cref{obj:ass:beta-order-polynomial}, and \cref{obj:ass:round-mean-variance-envelope}},\\
\text{and } \mathbb E_\pi[\bar Y_j]=m_P(p_j) \text{ for every } j=0,\ldots,k
\end{array}
\right\}.
\]
\end{definitionv}

Thus \leanref{sym:P_beta}{$\mathcal P_\beta$} is a finite-population law class indexed by the common polynomial order and variance scale, with the schedule entering through the evaluation points \(p_j\). The condition \(\mathbb E_\pi[\bar Y_j]=m_P(p_j)\) is the bridge between the randomization distribution and the deterministic polynomial approximation problem.

We estimate the endpoint contrast with a linear rollout estimator
\[
\hat\tau_{w,p}=\sum_{j=0}^k w_j\bar Y_j,
\]
where \leanref{sym:w}{$w$} is a vector of weights applied to the observed round means. Unbiasedness over all degree-\(\beta\) rollout mean curves is equivalent to exactness on the monomial basis.

\begin{definitionv}{def:unbiased-weight-set}[Unbiased weight set $W_\beta(p)$]
For a schedule \(p=(p_0,\ldots,p_k)\), the set of unbiased weights of order \(\beta\) is
\[
W_\beta(p)
=
\left\{
w\in\mathbb R^{k+1}:
\sum_{j=0}^k w_j p_j^0=0
\ \text{and}\
\sum_{j=0}^k w_j p_j^\ell=1
\ \text{for every } \ell=1,\ldots,\beta
\right\}.
\]
\end{definitionv}

The first equation in \cref{obj:def:unbiased-weight-set} removes the intercept \(m_P(0)\). The remaining equations reproduce the contribution of each nonconstant monomial to \(m_P(1)-m_P(0)\), so any \(w\in W_\beta(p)\) gives a design-unbiased linear estimator for every law in \cref{obj:def:rollout-law-class}, whenever the set is nonempty.

The schedules compared in the main theorem are shifted Chebyshev-Lobatto schedules. They place more measurements near the endpoints of the observed interval, the usual geometry for controlling polynomial extrapolation.

\begin{definitionv}{def:chebyshev-schedule}[Chebyshev schedule \(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}(k,q)\)]
For \(j=0,\ldots,k\), the shifted Chebyshev-Lobatto node on \([0,q]\) is
\[
p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_j(k,q)
=
\frac{q}{2}\left\{1-\cos\left(\frac{\pi j}{k}\right)\right\}.
\]
The shifted Chebyshev-Lobatto schedule is
\[
p^{\mathrm{Ch}}(k,q)
=
\left(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_0(k,q),\ldots,p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_k(k,q)\right).
\]
\end{definitionv}

We write \(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}\) for this schedule when \(k\) and \(q\) are clear. For comparison with a more elementary design, the equally spaced \(\beta+1\)-node schedule is \(p^{\mathrm{eq}}(\beta,q)\), with entries \(p^{\mathrm{eq}}_j(\beta,q)=qj/\beta\) for \(j=0,\ldots,\beta\).

The variance envelope makes the relevant design criterion the smallest possible \(\ell^1\) amplification among unbiased weights.

\begin{definitionv}{def:amplification-criterion}[Amplification criterion \(A_\beta(p)\)]
For a schedule \(p\), the amplification criterion is
\[
A_\beta(p)
=
\inf_{w\in W_\beta(p)}
\left(\sum_{j=0}^k |w_j|\right)^2.
\]
The minimax amplification over budgeted schedules is
\[
M_{\beta,k,q}
=
\inf_{p\in S_{k,q}} A_\beta(p).
\]
\end{definitionv}

The quantity \(A_\beta(p)\) records the best worst-case variance multiplier available at a fixed schedule under the total-variation variance envelope. The minimax quantity \(M_{\beta,k,q}\) then optimizes that multiplier over the admissible treated-fraction placements in \cref{obj:def:budgeted-schedule-class}.

Finally, it is useful to distinguish the envelope criterion from the exact finite-population covariance problem. For a law \(P\) and schedule \(p\), let \(\Gamma_P(p)\) be the design covariance matrix of the vector \((\bar Y_0,\ldots,\bar Y_k)\).

\begin{definitionv}{def:exact-nested-risk}[Exact nested risk \(R_{\mathrm{exact}}(\beta,k,q)\)]
The exact nested risk is
\[
R_{\mathrm{exact}}(\beta,k,q)
=
\inf_{p\in S_{k,q}}
\inf_{w\in W_\beta(p)}
\sup_{P\in\mathcal P_\beta}
w^\prime \Gamma_P(p) w,
\]
where \(\Gamma_P(p)\) is the design covariance matrix of the rollout round means under law \(P\) and schedule \(p\).
\end{definitionv}

\cref{obj:def:exact-nested-risk} leaves the true nested-rollout covariance matrix inside the minimax problem. The exact risk \(R_{\mathrm{exact}}\) is therefore the finite-population benchmark, while \cref{obj:def:amplification-criterion} is the sharp envelope problem solved in the main results.
