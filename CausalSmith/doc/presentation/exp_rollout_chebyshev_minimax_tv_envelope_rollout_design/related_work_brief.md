The paper sits in the potential-outcomes tradition in which interference is the central violation of the classical no-interference/SUTVA setup. Cox’s experimental-design formulation and Rubin’s potential-outcomes framework establish the baseline case in which each unit’s potential outcome depends only on its own treatment [@cox1958; @rubin1974; @imbens2015]. Manski’s work on social interactions and the reflection problem explains why spillovers and peer effects are not just nuisance dependence but identification objects in their own right [@manski1993; @manski2013]. The Horvitz-Thompson estimator supplies the canonical inverse-probability linear-unbiased template used throughout design-based interference work [@horvitz1952].

The modern interference literature has developed several ways to make causal effects estimable once outcomes may depend on others’ assignments. Aronow and Samii formalize general exposure mappings and randomization-based estimators under known interference structure [@aronow2017], while Sävje, Aronow, and Hudgens study what conventional estimators target when the interference structure is limited but unknown [@savje2017]. Eckles, Karrer, and Ugander and Ugander et al. emphasize graph cluster randomization and network exposure as tools for variance and bias control in network experiments [@eckles2017; @ugander2013]. Related strands develop exact and conditional randomization tests under interference [@athey2018; @basse2019; @puelz2019], sensitivity and graphical analyses [@vanderweele2015; @ogburn2014], and network g-computation approaches [@tchetgen2017].

The closest methodological lineage is the recent low-order network-interference program. Cortez, Eichhorn, and Yu show that staggered rollouts can identify the total treatment effect without observing the network when potential outcomes have bounded-order interactions, with the analysis reducing to polynomial extrapolation [@cortez2022]. Cortez-Rodriguez, Eichhorn, and Yu then analyze unit-randomized low-order estimators and minimax lower bounds [@cortezrodriguez2022], and the 2024 two-stage rollout paper studies clustering as a variance-reduction device for the same polynomial-interpolation estimator [@cortezrodriguez2024]. Eichhorn et al. extend the low-order framework to general clustered designs through a pseudoinverse estimator [@eichhorn2024], while Jiang and Wang, Cai et al., and Fan et al. explore mixed designs, independent-set designs, and regression-adjusted optimality under network interference [@jiang2023; @cai2023; @fan2025]. The proposed Chebyshev-spaced result differs from these papers by making the treated fractions themselves the design variable and by deriving the minimax linear-unbiased schedule and worst-case design-variance exponent over a Sobolev-ellipsoid coefficient class.

The design and approximation-theory side is equally central. Smith’s early polynomial-regression design problem, Kiefer-Wolfowitz optimum design theory, Kiefer’s equivalence theory, and Pukelsheim’s monograph provide the statistical-design language for choosing measurement locations rather than only analyzing a fixed design [@smith1918; @kiefer1959; @kiefer1974; @pukelsheim1993]. Karlin and Studden connect optimal design to Tchebycheff systems, while Rivlin and Trefethen give the Chebyshev extremal and interpolation results that make endpoint-spread Chebyshev nodes the natural candidate for minimax polynomial extrapolation [@karlin1966; @rivlin1990; @trefethen2013]. The Sobolev-ellipsoid and minimax-risk framing is aligned with the nonparametric estimation tradition summarized by Tsybakov and with recent ellipsoid-focused refinements of Pinsker-type theory [@tsybakov2009; @zhu2015; @allard2025]. Rollout work outside network interference, including staggered-rollout efficiency and cluster-randomized rollout inference, is adjacent but targets dynamic adoption timing rather than Chebyshev placement of treated-fraction measurement points for extrapolating the global treatment contrast [@roth2021; @chen2025].
