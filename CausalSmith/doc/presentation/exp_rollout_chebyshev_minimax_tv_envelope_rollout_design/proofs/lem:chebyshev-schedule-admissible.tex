\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{obj:lem:chebyshev-schedule-admissible}]
Let \(k\ge1\) and \(0<q\le1\). For
\[
p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_j(k,q)=\frac q2\left(1-\cos\frac{\pi j}{k}\right),
\]
the inequalities \(-1\le \cos(\pi j/k)\le1\) imply \(0\le 1-\cos(\pi j/k)\le2\). Since \(0<q\le1\), it follows that \(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_j(k,q)\in[0,1]\) for every \(j\). % lean: chebyshev_schedule_admissible
At \(j=0\), \(\cos 0=1\), so \(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_0(k,q)=0\). At \(j=k\), \(\cos\pi=-1\), so \(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_k(k,q)=q\). % lean: chebyshev_schedule_admissible
It remains to check strict increase. If \(0\le a<b\le k\), then
\[
0\le \frac{\pi a}{k}<\frac{\pi b}{k}\le \pi.
\]
Cosine is strictly decreasing on \([0,\pi]\), hence
\[
\cos\frac{\pi b}{k}<\cos\frac{\pi a}{k}.
\]
Thus
\[
1-\cos\frac{\pi a}{k}
<
1-\cos\frac{\pi b}{k},
\]
and multiplication by the positive number \(q/2\) gives
\[
p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_a(k,q)<p^{\mathrm{Ch}}_b(k,q).
\] % lean: chebyshev_schedule_admissible
The coordinates are therefore in \([0,1]\), start at \(0\), are strictly increasing, and end at \(q\), so \(p^{\mathrm{Ch}}(k,q)\in S_{k,q}\). % lean: chebyshev_schedule_admissible
\end{proof}
