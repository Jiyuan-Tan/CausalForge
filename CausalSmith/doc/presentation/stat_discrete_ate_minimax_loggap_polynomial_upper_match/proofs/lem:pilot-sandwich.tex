\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{obj:lem:pilot-sandwich}]
For a fixed category \(k\), the pilot count on \(I_0\) is exactly a Bernoulli count with success probability \(p_k\) and \(m_0=|I_0|\) trials.
If the sandwich event fails, then for some \(k\) either \(p_k<tL/(2m_0)\) while the pilot count exceeds \(tL\), or \(p_k>2tL/m_0\) while the pilot count is at most \(tL\).  This is just the definition of the pilot heavy set together with the floor inequalities.
The first alternative is bounded by the Bernoulli upper-tail estimate
\[
\exp\{-tL(\log2-1/2)\},
\]
and the second by the lower-tail estimate
\[
\exp\{-tL/4\}.
\]
A union bound over the \(d\) categories therefore gives, for \(n\ge2\) and \(t\ge0\),
\[
\Pr(\text{pilot bad})
\le
d\left[\exp\{-tL(\log2-1/2)\}+\exp\{-tL/4\}\right].
\]

Since \(\log2-1/2>0\), choose
\[
t_0=\max\left\{\frac{K+4}{\log2-1/2},\,4(K+4)\right\},\qquad C=2c,\qquad N=2 .
\]
For every \(t\ge t_0\), both exponential factors are at most \(\exp\{-(K+4)L\}\).  Using \(d\le cnL\), \(L\le n\), and \(\exp\{-q\log(en)\}=e^{-q}n^{-q}\), the preceding display is bounded by \(2c\,n^{-K}\).  Replacing the sample law by \(P^{\otimes n}\) under iid sampling proves the first assertion.

For the stated numerical specialization, take \(K=4\) and \(t=256\).  The inequalities \(8\le256(\log2-1/2)\) and \(8\le256/4\) satisfy the same threshold requirements, so the preceding argument gives constants \(C=2c\) and \(N=2\), after harmless enlargement, and hence the displayed \(n^{-4}\) bound.
\end{proof}
