\section{Setup and assumptions}

Let \leanref{S-1}{the observed unit} be
\(\leanref{sym:O}{$O=(Y,A,X)$}\), where the outcome is real-valued, the continuous treatment \leanref{sym:A}{$A$} takes values in \([0,1]\), and the covariate vector \leanref{sym:X}{$X$} takes values in \([0,1]^d\). The single-observation law is $P$. Throughout, \(d\) is fixed, and the sample consists of observations \(O_1,\ldots,O_n\) drawn from this law. We write
\[
\mu_P(a,x)=E_P[Y\mid A=a,X=x]
\]
for the treatment regression and
\[
\pi_P(a\mid x)
\]
for the conditional density of \(A\) given \(X=x\). The marginal law of \(X\) is \leanref{sym:P_X}{$P_X$}, with density \(p_{X,P}\) on \([0,1]^d\) whenever such a density is invoked below.

The sampling part of the experiment is the usual iid nonparametric experiment.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:iid-sampling}[Iid sampling]
The observations \(O_1,\ldots,O_n\) are iid draws from \(P\).
\end{assumptionv}

This is the standard iid sampling condition for minimax analysis over a nonparametric class \citep{tsybakov2009}. It fixes the experiment as the \(n\)-fold product law generated by the same one-observation distribution \(P\), leaving all restrictions on the law itself to the assumptions that follow.

For interpretation as a causal dose-response curve, the observed-data law is equipped with \leanref{S-2}{a potential-outcome causal overlay}. Thus \leanref{sym:Y}{$Y(a)$} denotes the potential outcome under exposure level \(a\), and the observed regression \(\mu_P\) is linked to a causal partial mean by the usual consistency and conditional ignorability restrictions.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:consistency}[Consistency]
Under \(P\),
\[
Y=Y(A)
\]
almost surely.
\end{assumptionv}

Consistency is the standard requirement that the observed outcome equals the potential outcome at the realized exposure level \citep{kennedy2017}. It rules out a mismatch between the observed response and the potential-outcome process, so that conditioning on \(A=a\) can be used to learn about outcomes under dose \(a\).

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:no-unmeasured-confounding}[No unmeasured confounding]
For every \(a\in[0,1]\), the potential outcome \(Y(a)\) is independent of \(A\) conditional on \(X\) under \(P\).
\end{assumptionv}

No unmeasured confounding is the standard conditional ignorability condition for continuous treatments \citep{kennedy2017}. Together with consistency, it gives the familiar identification route in which the dose-specific mean is represented through the conditional outcome regression averaged over the covariate distribution.

The lower-bound argument is local in the dose coordinate. We collect the boundedness, interiority, and positivity restrictions before imposing smoothness, because these conditions define the region on which the local perturbations are allowed to live.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:bounded-outcome}[Bounded outcome]
Under \(P\),
\[
|Y|\le M
\]
almost surely.
\end{assumptionv}

The bounded-outcome condition is a standard uniform envelope restriction \citep{bonvini2022}. It keeps the loss and the two-point outcome channels uniformly controlled over the model class.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:interior-dose}[Interior dose]
The exposure neighborhood around \(t_0\) is contained in the interior of the exposure support:
\[
[t_0-\varepsilon_0,t_0+\varepsilon_0]\subset(0,1).
\]
\end{assumptionv}

The evaluation dose \leanref{sym:t_0}{$t_0$} and radius \leanref{sym:epsilon_0}{$\varepsilon_0$} determine the \leanref{sym:evaluation window}{evaluation window} on which treatment-direction regularity is required. This is the standard interior evaluation point condition for continuous-exposure dose-response estimation \citep{colangelo2020}; it avoids boundary behavior at 0 and 1.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:local-positivity}[Local positivity]
For every \(a\in[t_0-\varepsilon_0,t_0+\varepsilon_0]\) and every \(x\in[0,1]^d\),
\[
\pi_P(a\mid x)\ge c_0.
\]
\end{assumptionv}

Local positivity is the standard overlap requirement near the target dose \citep{bonvini2022}. The constant \leanref{sym:c_0}{$c_0$} prevents the conditional treatment density from degenerating on the neighborhood where the functional is evaluated and where the lower-bound submodel is embedded.

For \(\gamma>0\) and a domain \(D\), let \(\mathcal H^\gamma(M;D)\) denote the Hölder ball of order \(\gamma\) and radius \leanref{sym:M}{$M$} on \(D\). The next assumptions state the smoothness imposed on the outcome regression, the conditional treatment density, and the covariate density. The treatment-coordinate orders are \leanref{sym:alpha}{$\alpha$} and \leanref{sym:beta}{$\beta$}; the covariate-coordinate order is \leanref{sym:s}{$s$}.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:mu-treatment-holder}[Treatment-regression dose smoothness]
For every \(x\in[0,1]^d\), the map
\[
a\longmapsto \mu_P(a,x)
\]
belongs to the Hölder ball \(\mathcal H^\alpha(M;[t_0-\varepsilon_0,t_0+\varepsilon_0])\).
\end{assumptionv}

This is the standard Hölder smoothness condition in the treatment coordinate for the outcome regression \citep{tsybakov2009}. It is the regularity that governs how sharply the conditional mean can vary as the dose approaches \(t_0\).

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:pi-treatment-holder}[Treatment-density dose smoothness]
For every \(x\in[0,1]^d\), the map
\[
a\longmapsto \pi_P(a\mid x)
\]
belongs to the Hölder ball \(\mathcal H^\beta(M;[t_0-\varepsilon_0,t_0+\varepsilon_0])\).
\end{assumptionv}

The conditional treatment density is also assumed to be Hölder smooth in the treatment coordinate, a standard regularity restriction in higher-order analyses of continuous treatments \citep{bonvini2022}. The order \(\beta\) will remain explicit in the model class, even though the lower-bound construction below keeps the treatment density fixed.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:mu-covariate-holder}[Treatment-regression covariate smoothness]
The map
\[
x\longmapsto \mu_P(t_0,x)
\]
belongs to the Hölder ball \(\mathcal H^s(M;[0,1]^d)\).
\end{assumptionv}

At the target dose, the outcome regression is required to be Hölder smooth in the covariate coordinate, a standard nonparametric smoothness condition \citep{tsybakov2009}. This restriction controls the complexity of the covariate-indexed signal that is averaged in the target functional.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:pi-covariate-holder}[Treatment-density covariate smoothness]
The map
\[
x\longmapsto \pi_P(t_0\mid x)
\]
belongs to the Hölder ball \(\mathcal H^s(M;[0,1]^d)\).
\end{assumptionv}

The matching covariate smoothness condition for the conditional treatment density is standard in continuous-treatment problems with higher-order nuisance structure \citep{bonvini2022}. It aligns the regularity of the density nuisance at \(t_0\) with the covariate smoothness scale used for the regression and covariate density.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:px-holder}[Covariate-density smoothness]
The marginal covariate density \(p_{X,P}\) belongs to the Hölder ball
\[
\mathcal H^s(M;[0,1]^d)
\]
and satisfies
\[
0 \le p_{X,P}(x) \le M
\]
for every \(x\in[0,1]^d\).
\end{assumptionv}

The Hölder-smooth covariate density condition is a standard density regularity assumption \citep{bonvini2022}. The additional upper bound by the common envelope keeps the marginal design distribution within the same radius convention as the other nuisance functions.

The final assumption is a construction-specific slack condition that places the least favorable submodel strictly inside the same smoothness, boundedness, and positivity restrictions.

\begin{assumptionv}{ass:baseline-submodel-slack}[Baseline submodel slack]
There exist a covariate density \(p_0\) on \([0,1]^d\), a conditional treatment density \(q_0\) on \([0,1]\), a constant \(\eta_0>0\), and a constant \(B_0\) with \(0<B_0<M\), such that \(p_0(x)\ge 0\) for all \(x\in[0,1]^d\), \(q_0(a)\ge 0\) for all \(a\in\mathbb R\),
\[
\int_{[0,1]^d} p_0(x)\,dx=1
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\int_0^1 q_0(a)\,da=1,
\]
\[
\|p_0\|_{\mathcal H^s([0,1]^d)}\le M-\eta_0,
\]
\(q_0\) has Hölder norm at most \(M-\eta_0\) of order \(\beta\) on \([t_0-\varepsilon_0,t_0+\varepsilon_0]\),
\[
q_0(a)\ge c_0+\eta_0
\qquad\text{for all }a\in[t_0-\varepsilon_0,t_0+\varepsilon_0],
\]
and
\[
p_0(x)\le M-\eta_0
\qquad\text{for all }x\in[0,1]^d.
\]
\end{assumptionv}

This slack requirement is specific to this analysis. It provides baseline densities \(p_0\) and \(q_0\) that remain a positive distance from the boundary of the class, leaving room for local perturbations while preserving the common Hölder radius, the covariate-density envelope, and the local positivity floor.

We now define the target parameter. Under the causal restrictions above, this is the covariate average of the conditional mean at the evaluation dose.

\begin{definitionv}{def:theta-functional}[Dose-response partial mean $\theta_P(t_0)$]
The dose-response partial mean at the evaluation dose $t_0$ is
\[
\theta_P(t_0)
=
\int_{[0,1]^d} \mu_P(t_0,x)\,p_{X,P}(x)\,dx .
\]
\end{definitionv}

\cref{obj:def:theta-functional} is the partial-mean form of the dose-response estimand: it holds the dose fixed at \(t_0\) and averages over the marginal covariate distribution under \(P\).

The same-class model used in the minimax statements is the collection of laws satisfying exactly the sampling, causal, boundedness, positivity, and Hölder restrictions just stated.

\begin{definitionv}{def:holder-dose-class}[Hölder Dose Class \(\mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0)\)]
With the minimax risk \(R_n(\mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0),t_0)\) and the published HOIF benchmark \(\rho_n\) as in \cref{obj:def:minimax-risk,obj:def:published-hoif-rate}, define
\[
\mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0)
\]
to be the collection of observed-data laws \(P\) satisfying all of the following conditions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{(Sampling.)} The observations satisfy \cref{obj:ass:iid-sampling}.
\item \textbf{(Causal identification.)} Consistency and no unmeasured confounding hold as in \cref{obj:ass:consistency,obj:ass:no-unmeasured-confounding}.
\item \textbf{(Outcome boundedness.)} The bounded-outcome condition holds as in \cref{obj:ass:bounded-outcome}.
\item \textbf{(Interior dose.)} The evaluation dose \(t_0\) and local radius \(\varepsilon_0\) satisfy \cref{obj:ass:interior-dose}.
\item \textbf{(Local positivity.)} The treatment density obeys the local positivity condition with constant \(c_0\) as in \cref{obj:ass:local-positivity}.
\item \textbf{(Treatment-direction smoothness.)} The maps \(a\mapsto \mu_P(a,x)\) and \(a\mapsto \pi_P(a\mid x)\) satisfy the treatment-direction Hölder conditions in \cref{obj:ass:mu-treatment-holder,obj:ass:pi-treatment-holder}.
\item \textbf{(Covariate-direction smoothness and density range.)} The maps \(x\mapsto\mu_P(t_0,x)\) and \(x\mapsto\pi_P(t_0\mid x)\) satisfy the covariate-direction Hölder conditions in \cref{obj:ass:mu-covariate-holder,obj:ass:pi-covariate-holder}, and \(p_{X,P}\) satisfies the Hölder, nonnegativity, and upper-envelope condition in \cref{obj:ass:px-holder}.
\item \textbf{(Semantic ties to the observed-data law.)} The regression, covariate-density, and treatment-density symbols are tied to \(P\): \(\mu_P(A,X)\) equals the conditional mean of \(Y\) given \((A,X)\) \(P\)-almost surely; \(p_{X,P}\) is the density of the \(X\)-marginal on \([0,1]^d\); and \(\pi_P\) is nonnegative on \([0,1]\times[0,1]^d\) with the \((A,X)\)-law density \((a,x)\mapsto \pi_P(a\mid x)p_{X,P}(x)\) relative to Lebesgue measure on \([0,1]\times[0,1]^d\).
\end{itemize}
\end{definitionv}

The definition deliberately uses one class for both the lower bound and the later comparison statements. In particular, the class itself includes the conditional treatment density smoothness indexed by \(\beta\).

For any estimator \(\hat\theta_n\) based on the sample, performance is measured by worst-case mean-squared error over this class.

\begin{definitionv}{def:minimax-risk}[Minimax risk $R_n(\mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0),t_0)$]
The minimax risk over the model class
\(\mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0)\) at the evaluation dose \(t_0\) is
\[
R_n\!\left(\mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0),t_0\right)
=
\inf_{\substack{\hat\theta_n\ \mathrm{measurable}\\
\hat\theta_n(O_1,\ldots,O_n)\in[-M,M]\ \mathrm{for\ every\ sample}}}
\sup_{P\in \mathcal P_{\alpha,\beta,s}(M,c_0,\varepsilon_0,t_0)}
E_{P^{\otimes n}}\!\left[
\left\{\hat\theta_n(O_1,\ldots,O_n)-\theta_P(t_0)\right\}^2
\right],
\]
where \(P^{\otimes n}\) is the product law of the iid sample \(O_1,\ldots,O_n\).
\end{definitionv}

The range restriction on \(\hat\theta_n\) is harmless for the bounded problem and keeps the estimator class aligned with the common envelope. The quantity in \cref{obj:def:minimax-risk} is the object lower bounded in the main results.

Finally, we record the published higher-order influence-function benchmark used only for comparison.

\begin{definitionv}{def:published-hoif-rate}[Published HOIF benchmark $\rho_n$]
The published higher-order influence-function benchmark rate is
\[
\rho_n
=
n^{-2\alpha/(2\alpha+1)}
\vee
n^{-2/(1+d/(4s)+1/\alpha)} .
\]
\end{definitionv}

Here \(\vee\) denotes the larger of the two displayed rates. The first term is the one-dimensional treatment-regression rate, while the second is the published low-covariate-smoothness benchmark used later to interpret the lower bound.
