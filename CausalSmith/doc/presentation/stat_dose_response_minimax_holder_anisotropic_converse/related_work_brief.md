The paper sits at the intersection of potential-outcome causal inference, semiparametric efficiency, and nonparametric minimax theory. The causal estimand is in the Rubin-Robins tradition: potential outcomes formalize counterfactual dose-specific means [rubin1974], while propensity-score and g-method arguments motivate adjustment under ignorability and positivity [rosenbaum1983; robins1986]. For continuous or non-binary exposures, the foundational econometric references are Imbens's dose-response propensity-score note [imbens2000], the generalized propensity score of Hirano and Imbens [hirano2004], and the propensity-function formulation of Imai and van Dyk [imai2004], with Zhao, van Dyk, and Imai providing a useful comparison of those non-binary treatment strategies [zhao2013].

Methodologically, the target $\theta(t)=E\{\mu(t,X)\}$ is a nonparametric partial-mean/dose-response functional rather than a finite-dimensional ATE. Classical semiparametric theory supplies the language of influence functions, regularity, and efficiency [bickel1993; vanderVaart1998; vanderLaan2003], while the modern orthogonal-score and cross-fitting literature explains why first-order doubly robust estimators can tolerate flexible nuisance estimation [chernozhukov2018]. Kennedy, Ma, McHugh, and Small [kennedy2017] is the key direct antecedent for nonparametric doubly robust estimation of a continuous-treatment effect curve; Lee [lee2018] and Colangelo and Lee [colangelo2020] develop related econometric partial-mean and double/debiased machine-learning analyses for continuous treatments.

The closest upper-bound paper is Bonvini and Kennedy [bonvini2022], which studies global and local dose-response estimation and constructs a higher-order influence-function estimator whose reported rate is the benchmark this lower-bound result matches. That work draws directly on the HOIF/minimax program of Robins, Li, Tchetgen Tchetgen, and van der Vaart [robins2008] and later higher-order estimating-equation developments [robins2015], where U-statistic corrections are used to improve over first-order bias-variance tradeoffs in low-smoothness semiparametric problems. The present lower bound is therefore positioned as the converse side of that rate story: it does not introduce a new estimator, but shows that under the anisotropic Holder structure in treatment, treatment-density, and covariate directions, the Bonvini-Kennedy HOIF rate cannot generally be improved for the interior dose-response curve.

The minimax component belongs to the standard nonparametric lower-bound tradition: Stone [stone1982] and Tsybakov [tsybakov2009] provide canonical rate and testing-prior machinery, while Goldenshluger and Lepski [goldenshluger2020] is a recent example of lower-bound analysis for anisotropic smoothness and nonlinear density functionals. Recent frontier papers address neighboring, but distinct, continuous-exposure questions. Doss et al. [doss2022], Hudson et al. [hudson2023], and Shi et al. [shi2024] study nonparametric inference or testing for dose-response curves; Hejazi et al. [hejazi2022] treats modified treatment policy effects based on generalized propensity scores; Zhang, Chen, and Giessing [zhang2024] relax positivity by changing the identifying structure; Schindl, Shen, and Kennedy [schindl2024] derive minimax bounds for incremental continuous-exposure interventions and limiting edge/interior tilts. Those papers are important comparators, but their estimands or positivity regimes differ from the interior standard dose-response partial mean considered here.
