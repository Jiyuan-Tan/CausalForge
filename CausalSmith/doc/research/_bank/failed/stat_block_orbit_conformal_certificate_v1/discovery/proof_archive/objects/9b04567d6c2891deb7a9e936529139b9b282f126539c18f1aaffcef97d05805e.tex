By \(\texttt{def:pair-action}\), \(s_b\) interchanges the two members of block \(b\) without changing the arm label and fixes every member outside that block. Thus the two states displayed in each \(O_{b,a}\) are mutually reachable, while no state in another block or arm is reachable. They are exactly the block's two arm-specific orbits.

If the target arm-\(1\) lifted law is supported on \(O_{b,1}\), its \((1,O_{b,1})\) orbit coordinate equals one. A mate-control analogue supported on the realized mate's control state lies in \(O_{b,0}\subseteq\mathcal U_0\) and has coordinate zero at \((1,O_{b,1})\). Its orbit vector therefore differs from the target vector and cannot satisfy the corresponding equation in the stacked common-selector linear program, equivalently the joint-balance equality in \(\texttt{def:joint-orbit-balance}\). It is also not a legal arm-\(1\) selector, because an arm-\(1\) integrated selector law must be supported on \(\mathcal U_1\).

Now impose the logically separate condition that a legal implemented arm-\(1\) selector satisfies \(q^{\mathrm{obs}}_{w,1}(O_{b,1})=0\). Since \(O_{b,1}\) is the only target-supported arm-\(1\) orbit, every target-supported orbit has zero calibration mass. This is precisely the arm-\(1\) premise of \(\texttt{prop:disjoint-orbit-witness}\), which gives zero coverage when \(k\le M\). If \(k=M+1\), \(\texttt{prop:rank-boundary}\) instead gives \(\mathcal C_w=\mathbb R\) and coverage one.
