Fix \(a\) and suppose \(k_a=M+1\). The convention in \(\texttt{def:rank-inversion}\) gives \(r_{a,(k_a)}=+\infty\). Thus its un-clipped residual interval is \(\mathbb R\), and clipping gives
\[
I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{a,w}=\mathbb R\cap[-K,K]=[-K,K].
\tag{1}
\]
If exactly arm zero is at the boundary, substitution of \((1)\) into the definition of the Minkowski difference gives
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{1,w}-[-K,K].
\]
If exactly arm one is at the boundary, the same substitution gives
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=[-K,K]-I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{0,w}.
\]
These are the exact one-arm boundary sets; no assertion that either equals \([-2K,2K]\) is made.

If both arms are at the boundary, \((1)\) holds twice and therefore
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=[-K,K]-[-K,K]=[-2K,2K].
\]
The bounded-potential-outcome assumption implies
\[
-2K\le Y_{0T}(1)-Y_{0T}(0)=\tau_T\le2K
\]
almost surely. Hence the last set covers with conditional probability one. On the diagonal \(\varepsilon_0=\varepsilon_1=\alpha/2\), \(\texttt{lem:armwise-finite-rank-staircase}\) gives \(k_0=k_1=k\), so a boundary necessarily occurs in both arms and the symmetric proposition follows.
