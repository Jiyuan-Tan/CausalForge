Put \(N=M+1\). For either arm \(c\), integrality gives
\[
k_c\le M\Longleftrightarrow \lceil N(1-\varepsilon_c)\rceil\le N-1\Longleftrightarrow N(1-\varepsilon_c)\le N-1\Longleftrightarrow \varepsilon_c\ge\frac1{M+1}.
\tag{1}
\]
The orbit-constant construction, invariance, boundedness, and experimental-law verification are exactly those established in \texttt{prop:disjoint-orbit-witness}; they concern the actual \(\mathcal G\)-orbits and do not use a pair specialization. In particular, the constructed law lies in \(\mathcal P_{\mathcal G,K}\), every selected calibration score in both arms is zero, and
\[
\tau_T=\begin{cases}K,&a=1,\\-K,&a=0.\end{cases}
\tag{2}
\]
By \texttt{def:rank-inversion}, a zero calibration sample gives
\[
r_{c,(k_c)}=0\quad \text{if }k_c\le M,\qquad r_{c,(k_c)}=+\infty\quad \text{if }k_c=M+1.
\tag{3}
\]
If both ranks are finite, (3) gives \(I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{0,w}=I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{1,w}=\{0\}\), and hence
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=\{0\}.
\tag{4}
\]
Since \(K>0\), (2) and (4) give coverage zero. If exactly one rank equals \(M+1\), \texttt{prop:asymmetric-rank-boundary} and (3) give one interval \([-K,K]\) and the other interval \(\{0\}\). In either order their Minkowski difference is \([-K,K]\), which contains both possible values in (2), so this witness has coverage one. If both ranks equal \(M+1\), the same boundary proposition gives \(\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=[-2K,2K]\), again containing (2) almost surely. Equation (1) proves the stated rank-regime equivalence. On the diagonal \(\varepsilon_0=\varepsilon_1=\alpha/2\), the two ranks coincide and the two possible regimes reduce to \texttt{prop:disjoint-orbit-witness} and its boundary case.
