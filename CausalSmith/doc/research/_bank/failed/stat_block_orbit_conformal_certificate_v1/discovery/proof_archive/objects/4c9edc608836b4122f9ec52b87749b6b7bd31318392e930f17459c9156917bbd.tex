Fix \(a\in\{0,1\}\) and suppose \(k_a=M+1\). The convention in \(\texttt{def:rank-inversion}\) gives \(r_{a,(k_a)}=+\infty\). Its un-clipped residual interval is therefore \(\mathbb R\), and clipping gives
\[
I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{a,w}=\mathbb R\cap[-K,K]=[-K,K].
\tag{1}
\]
If only arm zero is at the boundary, substitution of (1) into the defining Minkowski difference yields
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{1,w}-[-K,K].
\]
If only arm one is at the boundary, it similarly yields
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=[-K,K]-I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{0,w}.
\]
These identities are exact and do not require either one-boundary set to equal \([-2K,2K]\).

If both arms are at the boundary, (1) holds twice, and thus
\[
\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=[-K,K]-[-K,K]=[-2K,2K].
\]
By \(\texttt{ass:bounded-potential-outcomes}\),
\[
-2K\le Y_{0T}(1)-Y_{0T}(0)=\tau_T\le2K
\]
almost surely, so this last set has conditional coverage one for every law and every fixed trained predictor. On the diagonal \(\varepsilon_0=\varepsilon_1=\alpha/2\), \(\texttt{lem:armwise-finite-rank-staircase}\) gives \(k_0=k_1=k\). Hence a boundary occurs in both arms or in neither, and the displayed conclusions reduce to the symmetric boundary proposition.
