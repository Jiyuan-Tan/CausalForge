Put \(N=M+1\). First draw \(X_0,\ldots,X_M\) independently from \(Q\), write \(W_j=\lambda(E_j)\), and let \(W=\sum_{j=0}^M W_j\). For each candidate index \(j\), define \(q_j\) by placing weight \(W_\ell\) at \(R_\ell\) for \(\ell\ne j\), placing weight \(W_j\) at \(+\infty\), normalizing by \(W\), and taking the left-continuous \((1-\epsilon)\)-quantile. When \(W=0\), define the candidate event below to be false. Put \(A_j=\{R_j>q_j\}\).

We first prove the deterministic inequality
\[
\sum_{j=0}^M W_j\mathbf 1_{A_j}\le\epsilon W.
\tag{1}
\]
If no \(A_j\) occurs, this is immediate. Otherwise choose an index \(j_*\) with minimal \(R_j\) among the occurring events. On \(A_{j_*}\), the quantile \(q_{j_*}\) is finite and \(q_{j_*}<R_{j_*}\). By the definition of the left-continuous quantile, the mass at finite scores not exceeding \(q_{j_*}\) is at least \(1-\epsilon\). Consequently,
\[
W_{j_*}+\sum_{\substack{\ell\ne j_*\\R_\ell>q_{j_*}}}W_\ell\le\epsilon W.
\tag{2}
\]
Every index \(j\) for which \(A_j\) occurs has \(R_j\ge R_{j_*}>q_{j_*}\). Thus all weights on the left side of (1) are included on the left side of (2), proving (1). This argument also handles ties because the reported event uses strict exceedance.

The law \(P\) has density \(W_0\) relative to \(Q\) in coordinate zero, since \(dP/dQ(X_0)=W_0\). Therefore the probability in the lemma equals
\[
\mathbb E_{Q^N}[W_0\mathbf 1_{A_0}].
\tag{3}
\]
The candidate construction is permutation equivariant and \(Q^N\) is exchangeable, so
\[
\mathbb E_{Q^N}[W_0\mathbf 1_{A_0}]=\frac1N\mathbb E_{Q^N}\left[\sum_{j=0}^M W_j\mathbf 1_{A_j}\right]\le\frac{\epsilon}{N}\mathbb E_{Q^N}[W].
\tag{4}
\]
Finally, \(\mathbb E_Q[W_j]=\int(dP/dQ)\,dQ=1\), and hence \(\mathbb E_{Q^N}[W]=N\). Substitution into (4) gives \(\Pr_P\{R_0>q_{1-\epsilon}\}\le\epsilon\), as required.
