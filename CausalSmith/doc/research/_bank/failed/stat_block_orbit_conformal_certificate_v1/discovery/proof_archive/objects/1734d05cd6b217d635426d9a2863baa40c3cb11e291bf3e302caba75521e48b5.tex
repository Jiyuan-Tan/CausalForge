Put \(N=M+1\). For either arm \(c\), integrality gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
k_c\le M&\Longleftrightarrow \lceil N(1-\varepsilon_c)\rceil\le N-1\\
&\Longleftrightarrow N(1-\varepsilon_c)\le N-1\\
&\Longleftrightarrow \varepsilon_c\ge\frac1{M+1}.
\end{aligned}
\tag{1}
\]
The orbit-constant construction, invariance, boundedness, and experimental-law verification are those established in \(\texttt{prop:disjoint-orbit-witness}\). In particular,
\[
P\in\mathcal P_{\mathcal G,K},\qquad S_{i,0}=S_{i,1}=0\quad(1\le i\le M),
\tag{2}
\]
and
\[
\tau_T=\begin{cases}K,&a=1,\\-K,&a=0.\end{cases}
\tag{3}
\]
By \(\texttt{def:rank-inversion}\), a zero calibration sample gives
\[
r_{c,(k_c)}=\begin{cases}0,&k_c\le M,\\+\infty,&k_c=M+1.\end{cases}
\tag{4}
\]
If both ranks are finite, (4) gives \(I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{0,w}=I^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_{1,w}=\{0\}\) and \(\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=\{0\}\). Since \(K>0\), (3) gives zero conditional coverage. If exactly one rank equals \(M+1\), \(\texttt{prop:asymmetric-rank-boundary}\) and (4) give one arm interval \([-K,K]\) and the other \(\{0\}\). In either order their Minkowski difference is
\[
[-K,K]-\{0\}=\{0\}-[-K,K]=[-K,K],
\tag{5}
\]
which contains both values in (3), so coverage is one. If both ranks equal \(M+1\), the same proposition gives \(\mathcal C^{\boldsymbol\varepsilon}_w=[-2K,2K]\), again containing (3). Finally, on the diagonal \(\varepsilon_0=\varepsilon_1=\alpha/2\), the two ranks coincide by \(\texttt{def:rank-inversion}\); the finite-rank case and the two-boundary case reduce to \(\texttt{prop:disjoint-orbit-witness}\) and its boundary conclusion.
