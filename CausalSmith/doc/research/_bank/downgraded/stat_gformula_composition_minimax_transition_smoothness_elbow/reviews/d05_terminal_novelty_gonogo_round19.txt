### 1. Primary-source theorem audit

Source: [Robins et al. (2009), publisher PDF](/tmp/robins2009_ejs479.pdf).

Theorem 2.1, journal p. 1307:

- Partition \(\mathcal X=\bigcup_{j=1}^k\mathcal X_j\).
- For \(\lambda=(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_k)\), require
  \[
  P_\lambda(\mathcal X_j)=Q_\lambda(\mathcal X_j)=p_j,
  \]
  independently of \(\lambda\), and require the restrictions of \(P_\lambda,Q_\lambda\) to \(\mathcal X_j\) to depend only on \(\lambda_j\).
- For densities \(p_\lambda,q_\lambda\), product prior \(\pi=\bigotimes_j\pi_j\), and
  \[
  p=\int p_\lambda\,d\pi,\qquad q=\int q_\lambda\,d\pi,
  \]
  define
  \[
  a=\max_j\sup_\lambda\frac1{p_j}\int_{\mathcal X_j}
       \frac{(p_\lambda-p)^2}{p_\lambda}\,d\mu,
  \]
  \[
  b=\max_j\sup_\lambda\frac1{p_j}\int_{\mathcal X_j}
       \frac{(q_\lambda-p_\lambda)^2}{p_\lambda}\,d\mu,
  \]
  \[
  d=\max_j\sup_\lambda\frac1{p_j}\int_{\mathcal X_j}
       \frac{(q-p)^2}{p_\lambda}\,d\mu.
  \]
- If \(np_j(1\vee a\vee b)\le A\) for every \(j\), and \(p_\lambda\) is uniformly bounded above and away from zero, then
  \[
  \rho\!\left(\int P_\lambda^n\,d\pi,\int Q_\lambda^n\,d\pi\right)
  \ge
  1-Cn^2\max_jp_j\,(b^2+ab)-Cnd.
  \]
  The constant depends only on the displayed uniform constants.

The proof is through the cellwise affinity factorization in Lemma 5.1, equation (5.1), journal pp. 1315–1316, and the two-mixture bound in Lemma 5.2, equation (5.2), pp. 1317–1319. This is expressly a theorem for perturbing two different functional parameters; it is not limited to a product-versus-mixture construction.

Section 3 parameterizes the MAR model by
\[
a(z)^{-1}=P(A=1\mid Z=z),\qquad
b(z)=P(Y=1\mid Z=z),\qquad
g(z)=\frac{f(z)}{a(z)}.
\]
Here \(g\) is not a normalized conditional density. It is
\[
g(z)=P(A=1,Z\in dz)/d\nu(z),
\qquad \int g\,d\nu=P(A=1).
\]
The identified mean is
\[
\chi=\int a(z)b(z)g(z)\,d\nu(z)=\int f(z)b(z)\,d\nu(z).
\]
These definitions are on journal p. 1309.

The smaller model \(P_1\) fixes \(g=1/2\), imposes \(C^\alpha\) and \(C^\beta\) bounds on \(a\) and \(b\), and keeps \(a^{-1}\) and \(b\) uniformly away from zero and one. Consequently \(f=ag=a/2\), not \(f=g\).

Theorem 3.1, journal p. 1310:

> If \((\alpha+\beta)/2<d/4\), the minimax rate over \(P_1\) for estimating \(\int abg\,d\nu\) is at least
> \[
> n^{-(2\alpha+2\beta)/(2\alpha+2\beta+d)}.
> \]

Its proof uses \(k\asymp n^{2d/(2\alpha+2\beta+d)}\) disjoint cells and
\[
a_\lambda=2+k^{-\alpha/d}\sum_i\lambda_iH_i,\qquad
b_\lambda=\frac12+k^{-\beta/d}\sum_i\lambda_iH_i.
\]
Because \(g=1/2\),
\[
f_\lambda=\frac{a_\lambda}{2},
\qquad
f_\lambda P(A=1\mid Z)=\frac{a_\lambda}{2}\frac1{a_\lambda}=\frac12.
\]
Thus the complete-case covariate subdensity is fixed. The null-side functional is \(1/2\), while the jointly perturbed side is
\[
\frac12+\frac12k^{-(\alpha+\beta)/d}
\]
(journal pp. 1310–1311). Equation (3.2) gives the positive-affinity calibration. The proof treats both \(\alpha\le\beta\) and \(\alpha\ge\beta\) by reversing which parameter appears in the first mixture.

### 2. Legal embedding into the present model

Set \(\alpha=\alpha_1\) and \(\beta=t\).

| Present coordinate/constraint | Embedded construction |
|---|---|
| \(H_1\) | \(H_1=Z\), with density \(f_\lambda=a_\lambda/2\). |
| First propensity | \(\pi_{1,\lambda}(z)=1/a_\lambda(z)\). |
| \(H_2\mid H_1=z,A_1=1\) | \((1-b_\lambda(z))k_0+b_\lambda(z)k_1\). |
| Binary sufficient region | Take \(R_0=\{h_{2,1}\le1/2\}\), \(R_1=R_0^c\), \(k_0=2\mathbf1_{R_0}\), \(k_1=2\mathbf1_{R_1}\). Then \(W=\mathbf1_{R_1}(H_2)\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(b_\lambda(z))\). |
| Fixed terminal regression | Take \(m_2(h_2)=\delta(2h_{2,1}-1)\), with sufficiently small fixed \(\delta>0\). It is analytic and hence belongs to every finite \(C^\beta\) class. |
| Other channels | Fix \(H_2\mid A_1=0\), \(\pi_2=1/2\), and all outcome kernels except the fixed treated kernel determined by \(m_2\). |

The two component expectations are
\[
\mu_0=\int m_2k_0=-\delta/2,\qquad
\mu_1=\int m_2k_1=\delta/2.
\]
Therefore
\[
m_{1,\lambda}(z)
=\int m_2(h_2)g_\lambda(h_2\mid z)\,dh_2
=-\frac{\delta}{2}+\delta b_\lambda(z),
\]
and
\[
\psi_\lambda
=\int f_\lambda(z)m_{1,\lambda}(z)\,dz
=-\frac{\delta}{2}
+\delta\int f_\lambda(z)b_\lambda(z)\,dz
=-\frac{\delta}{2}+\delta\chi_\lambda.
\]
Target separation is thus exactly the 2009 separation multiplied by the fixed nonzero constant \(\delta\):
\[
|\Delta\psi|
=\frac{\delta}{2}k^{-(\alpha_1+t)/d}
\asymp
n^{-2(\alpha_1+t)/(2(\alpha_1+t)+d)}
=n^{-r_1}.
\]

All present-model restrictions are met:

- Incoming smoothness:
  \[
  g_\lambda(\cdot\mid z)-g_\lambda(\cdot\mid z')
  =[b_\lambda(z)-b_\lambda(z')](k_1-k_0),
  \]
  so the \(C^t(L^1)\) norm is controlled by the \(C^t\) norm of \(b_\lambda\). Small amplitudes use the \(M>1\) slack.
- Propensity smoothness: inversion preserves \(C^{\alpha_1}\) smoothness because \(a_\lambda\) stays uniformly away from zero.
- Fixed \(m_2\): analytic, bounded, and independent of \(\lambda\).
- Strict overlap: \(a_\lambda\) remains close to \(2\), so \(\pi_1\) remains close to \(1/2\); set \(\pi_2=1/2\).
- \(Q_1\): \(f_\lambda=a_\lambda/2\) stays bounded above and away from zero.
- \(Q_2\): writing \(B_\lambda=\int f_\lambda b_\lambda\),
  \[
  q_{2,\lambda}=(1-B_\lambda)k_0+B_\lambda k_1.
  \]
  Since \(b_\lambda\) is bounded away from zero and one, so is \(B_\lambda\), yielding fixed upper and lower density bounds.
- State sufficiency: the treated outcome kernel depends on history only through \(H_2\); \(A_2\) is fixed fair treatment depending on no omitted history.
- Causal extension: independent uniforms can generate \(A_1\), \(H_2(a_1)\), \(A_2\), and potential outcomes. Separate randomizers give consistency and both sequential exchangeability statements.
- Observed experiment: when \(A_1=1\), \(W=\mathbf1_{R_1}(H_2)\) is exactly the paper’s observed Bernoulli response. Conditional on \(W\), the remaining location of \(H_2\) is drawn from fixed \(k_W\); when \(A_1=0\), all added coordinates are fixed-parameter ancillary channels. Conversely, the paper’s observation is recovered as
  \[
  (A_1W,A_1,H_1).
  \]
  Hence the experiments are Blackwell-equivalent, not merely asymptotically similar.

In the non-root regime \(\alpha_1+t<d/2\), this is directly Theorem 3.1. When \(\alpha_1+t\ge d/2\), \(n^{-r_1}\le n^{-1/2}\), so that term supplies nothing beyond the already standard parametric floor.

### 3. Variation dependence

Variation dependence does not block transfer.

It is true that the present \(m_1\) cannot be perturbed independently of \(g\), because \(m_1=T_gm_2\). But the reduction legally realizes the paper’s \(b_\lambda\) as a transition mixture weight:
\[
T_{g_\lambda}m_2=\mu_0+(\mu_1-\mu_0)b_\lambda.
\]
Thus the constraint is satisfied identically. It changes the representation of the hard family, not its information geometry or rate.

The later noncoverage result concerning a positive-order smooth joint \((L_0,L_1)\) density does not rescue novelty. The 2009 theorem requires no such longitudinal joint-density condition, and the embedding above deliberately permits the same rough outgoing densities allowed by the present \(C^t(L^1)\) transition model.

### 4. Novelty-tier consequence

The field-level novelty claim fails:

- \(n^{-r_1}\) is a corollary/embedding of Theorem 3.1 in the only regime where it can dominate the parametric floor.
- \(n^{-r_2}\) is already acknowledged as the standard terminal channel.
- The \(n^{-1/2}\) floor is standard.
- The upper bound is first-order and remains unmatched with the lower bound.
- No minimax rate or sharp elbow is proved.
- Rough-outgoing noncoverage of a later theorem establishes only noncoverage of that theorem’s assumptions, not noncoverage of the earlier MAR lower construction.

At most, the note contains a model-specific causal realization and exposition of an existing lower-bound mechanism. That is below the stated FIELD threshold.
