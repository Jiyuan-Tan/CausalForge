\section*{stat_neyman_regret_minimax (adaptive two-arm experiment; first matching minimax lower bound pinning the sharp minimax Neyman-regret rate on a tangent-regular bounded-outcome class)}

\paragraph{TL;DR.} First matching-RATE minimax LOWER bound for superpopulation Horvitz-Thompson cumulative Neyman regret, resolving the open logarithmic converse: the O(log T) upper bounds of Neopane-Ramdas-Singh (2024) and Noarov et al. (2025) lacked a matching lower bound for this objective. The main theorem lower-bounds expected cumulative Neyman regret by a log T term times a closed-form local curvature-information complexity kappa_nu (S^2-scaled), and the global corollary gives an inf-sup Omega(log T) minimax rate on a named interior moment/overlap + strong-identification class. The universal RATE is log T; the fixed class radii control only the frontier CONSTANT K, whose closed-form moment envelope is solved here. The only remaining open object (OEQ) is the constant-sharp minimax value (a matching constant-sharp UPPER bound). This is the superpopulation Horvitz-Thompson converse, distinct from Chen-Ge-Qian-Harshaw's neighboring design-based AIPW upper/lower minimax program.

\section{Project justification}

\paragraph{Gap.} The literature now has O(log T) upper bounds for adaptive Neyman regret, competitive analyses built around learning unknown arm standard deviations, and even a neighboring design-based AIPW minimax lower bound, but no matching lower bound for the same superpopulation Horvitz-Thompson cumulative variance-regret objective. Nearby converses otherwise target treatment-choice welfare regret or nonadaptive off-policy evaluation risk.

\paragraph{Niche.} The open niche is a theorem that speaks directly to estimator variance inefficiency, not to chosen-arm welfare, and that names the local information ratio making logarithmic regret unavoidable on a well-posed tangent-regular class.

\paragraph{Fill.} The project fills that niche by turning second-moment learning delay into cumulative Neyman loss through bounded-outcome mean-preserving alternatives, producing both an instancewise complexity handle kappa_nu and a global Omega(log T) corollary on compact regular subclasses.

\section{Related work}

Neyman (1934) and Horvitz-Thompson (1952) supply the oracle allocation and inverse-probability variance object behind the regret functional. Dai, Gradu, and Harshaw (2023, Section 3) introduced Neyman regret for adaptive Horvitz-Thompson estimation in the design-based setting and proved the first O~(sqrt(T)) upper bound, but not a converse. Neopane, Ramdas, and Singh (2024, abstract and Section 1, lines 20-24 of arXiv v1) showed that ClipSMT improves the superpopulation upper rate to O(log T), again from the upper-bound side only. Noarov, Fogliato, Bertran, and Roth (2025, Theorem 3.2 and equation (1)) obtained an anytime O~(log T) design-based upper bound for ClipOGDSC under lower-bounded outcomes, which sharpens the rate benchmark but still leaves the minimax lower bound open for the superpopulation Horvitz-Thompson objective. Zhao (2023, abstract) isolates the core obstacle as learning arm standard deviations across stages and gives competitive upper guarantees, making it the closest positive comparator on the variance-learning bottleneck even though it does not study cumulative adaptive Horvitz-Thompson variance regret or minimax converses. Kato, Ishihara, Honda, and Narita (2020/2025) study efficient adaptive experimental design for ATE estimation from the achievability side, but do not formulate cumulative Neyman-regret minimax lower bounds. Kato (2025/2026) provides the closest adaptive lower-bound template via change-of-measure arguments for treatment-choice simple regret; the loss is welfare rather than cumulative variance inefficiency. Ma et al. (2021) provide the closest estimation-risk converse for inverse-weighted functionals, but their OPE lower bounds do not include an adaptive logging rule whose purpose is to learn pi_nu_star. Cook, Mishler, and Ramdas (2024) show that adaptive ATE estimators can still enjoy valid asymptotic inference, which sharpens the interpretation of Neyman regret as the nonasymptotic efficiency loss that precedes the inference layer. Chen, Ge, Qian, and Harshaw (arXiv:2511.19905, 2025, Sigmoid-FTRL) study a DESIGN-BASED (fixed potential outcomes) AIPW adaptive Neyman allocation and give both an upper-bound algorithm and a neighboring design-based minimax lower bound; our target is distinct: a SUPERPOPULATION Horvitz-Thompson cumulative variance-regret minimax LOWER bound plus a closed-form frontier, so ours is the first matching-rate converse for the Horvitz-Thompson superpopulation objective, differing in estimator (HT vs AIPW) and randomness model (superpopulation vs design-based).

\section{Assumptions}

\begin{assumption}[ass:bounded-outcomes]\label{ass:bounded-outcomes}
supp(nu) subseteq [0,1]^2.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Standard} (bounded-outcome superpopulation potential-outcomes model, \cite{neopane2024logarithmic}).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:superpopulation-iid]\label{ass:superpopulation-iid}
((Y_t(0), Y_t(1)))_{t=1}^T are i.i.d. with common law nu.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Standard} (i.i.d. superpopulation potential-outcomes sampling, \cite{neopane2024logarithmic}).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:predictable-design]\label{ass:predictable-design}
pi_t is H_{t-1}-measurable for each t in {1, ..., T}.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Standard} (nonanticipating adaptive design, \cite{dai2023clipogd}).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:bernoulli-randomization]\label{ass:bernoulli-randomization}
A_t | H_{t-1} ~ Bernoulli(pi_t) for each t in {1, ..., T}.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Standard} (adaptive Bernoulli randomization, \cite{dai2023clipogd}).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:interior-second-moments]\label{ass:interior-second-moments}
m_a > 0 for each a in {0,1}.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Standard} (positive armwise second moments for interior Neyman allocation, \cite{neopane2024logarithmic}).
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:arm-tangent-nondegeneracy]\label{ass:arm-tangent-nondegeneracy}
inf_{b_0,b_1 in R} int (y^2 - b_0 - b_1 y)^2 d nu_a(y) > 0 for each a in {0,1}.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Novel.} Standard second-moment (quadratic-curvature) NONDEGENERACY / strong-identification condition: y^2 is not a.s. affine in y (equivalently the arm law has >2 support points), so the local Fisher information for a second-moment perturbation is positive. This is the adaptive-experiment analogue of the standard non-degenerate-information (strong-identification) condition; it only makes the local complexity kappa_nu well-defined and positive and does NOT affect the log T rate.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:interior-band]\label{ass:interior-band}
underline_m <= m_a <= overline_m for each a in {0,1}.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Novel.} Standard INTERIOR MOMENT / OVERLAP band: m_a is bounded away from 0 and the boundary, the second-moment analogue of the overlap/positivity localization used in every minimax-over-a-class statement. The fixed radii underline_m, overline_m are named class constants that control only the frontier CONSTANT K; the minimax RATE is the universal log T.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ass:tangent-band]\label{ass:tangent-band}
underline_r <= inf_{b_0,b_1 in R} int (y^2 - b_0 - b_1 y)^2 d nu_a(y) for each a in {0,1}.
\par\smallskip\noindent\emph{Novel.} Standard TANGENT-STRENGTH / Fisher-information LOWER bound (r_a >= underline_r): the residual strength of y^2 after projection on span{1,y} is bounded below, the uniform strong-identification analogue for uniform minimax control. The radius underline_r is a named class constant controlling only the frontier CONSTANT K, not the log T rate.
\end{assumption}

\section{Definitions}

\begin{definition}[def:model-class]\label{def:model-class}
$M_int := {nu on [0,1]^2 : supp(nu) subseteq [0,1]^2 and m_a(nu) > 0 for each a in {0,1}}$ --- the class of all objects satisfying: ass:bounded-outcomes; ass:interior-second-moments.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:tangent-model-class]\label{def:tangent-model-class}
$M_tan := {nu in M_int : inf_{b_0,b_1 in R} int (y^2 - b_0 - b_1 y)^2 d nu_a(y) > 0 for each a in {0,1}}$ --- the class of all objects satisfying: ass:bounded-outcomes; ass:interior-second-moments; ass:arm-tangent-nondegeneracy.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:joint-law]\label{def:joint-law}
$P_{nu,Alg} := The law induced by ass:superpopulation-iid, ass:predictable-design, and ass:bernoulli-randomization for horizon T under instance nu and adaptive design Alg.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } nu, Alg, T)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:oracle-allocation]\label{def:oracle-allocation}
$pi_nu_star := pi_nu_star = m_1 / (m_0 + m_1).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } m_0, m_1)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:variance-objective]\label{def:variance-objective}
$V_nu := V_nu(pi) = m_1^2 / pi + m_0^2 / (1 - pi).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } m_0, m_1)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:neyman-gap]\label{def:neyman-gap}
$g_nu := g_nu(pi) = V_nu(pi) - V_nu(pi_nu_star).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } V_nu, pi_nu_star)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:algorithm-class]\label{def:algorithm-class}
$A_T := {Alg = (pi_1, ..., pi_T) : pi_t is H_{t-1}-measurable and A_t | H_{t-1} ~ Bernoulli(pi_t) for each t in {1, ..., T}}$ --- the class of all objects satisfying: ass:predictable-design; ass:bernoulli-randomization.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:cumulative-regret]\label{def:cumulative-regret}
$mathfrak_R_T(Alg,nu) := mathfrak_R_T(Alg,nu) = int [sum_{t=1}^T g_nu(pi_t)] d P_{nu,Alg}.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } Alg, g_nu, P_{nu,Alg}, T)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:arm-tangent-strengths]\label{def:arm-tangent-strengths}
$r_{a,nu} := For a in {0,1}, r_{a,nu} = inf_{b_0,b_1 in R} int (y^2 - b_0 - b_1 y)^2 d nu_a(y).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } nu_0, nu_1)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:arm-score-costs]\label{def:arm-score-costs}
$J_{a,nu}(u_a) := For a in {0,1}, J_{a,nu}(u_a) = inf { int s_a(y)^2 d nu_a(y) : int s_a(y) d nu_a(y) = 0, int y s_a(y) d nu_a(y) = 0, int y^2 s_a(y) d nu_a(y) = u_a }.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } nu_0, nu_1, u_0, u_1, s_0, s_1)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:local-information]\label{def:local-information}
$J_nu(u) := J_nu(u) = pi_nu_star J_{1,nu}(u_1) + (1 - pi_nu_star) J_{0,nu}(u_0).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } pi_nu_star, J_{0,nu}(u_0), J_{1,nu}(u_1), u)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:oracle-sensitivity]\label{def:oracle-sensitivity}
$dot_pi_nu(u) := dot_pi_nu(u) = (u_1 m_0 / m_1 - u_0 m_1 / m_0) / (2 (m_0 + m_1)^2).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } u_0, u_1, m_0, m_1)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:loss-curvature]\label{def:loss-curvature}
$H_nu := H_nu = (m_0 + m_1)^4 / (m_0 m_1).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } m_0, m_1)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:feasible-direction-set]\label{def:feasible-direction-set}
$U_nu := U_nu = {u in R^2 : 0 < J_nu(u) < infinity and dot_pi_nu(u) != 0}.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } J_nu(u), dot_pi_nu(u))
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:local-complexity]\label{def:local-complexity}
$kappa_nu := Let S_nu=m_0+m_1. Define kappa_nu = sup_{u in U_nu} S_nu^2 dot_pi_nu(u)^2 / J_nu(u). Equivalently, this corrected complexity equals pi_nu_star(1-pi_nu_star) times the curvature-scaled quotient previously written with H_nu.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } U_nu, H_nu, dot_pi_nu(u), J_nu(u))
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:local-path-handle]\label{def:local-path-handle}
$nu^(u,h) := Design a bounded-support path h -> nu^(u,h) through nu with nu^(u,0) = nu, int y d nu_a^(u,h)(y) = int y d nu_a(y), int y^2 d nu_a^(u,h)(y) = m_a^2 + h u_a + o(h), and KL(nu_a^(u,h), nu_a) = (h^2 / 2) J_{a,nu}(u_a) + o(h^2) for a in {0,1}.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } nu, u, h, J_{0,nu}(u_0), J_{1,nu}(u_1))
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:testing-scale]\label{def:testing-scale}
$h_T(nu,u) := h_T(nu,u) = (log T / (T J_nu(u)))^(1/2).$ \quad (\text{inputs: } T, J_nu(u))
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:regular-band-class]\label{def:regular-band-class}
$M(underline_m, overline_m, underline_r) := {nu in M_tan : underline_m <= m_a(nu) <= overline_m and underline_r <= inf_{b_0,b_1 in R} int (y^2 - b_0 - b_1 y)^2 d nu_a(y) for each a in {0,1}}$ --- the class of all objects satisfying: ass:bounded-outcomes; ass:interior-second-moments; ass:arm-tangent-nondegeneracy; ass:interior-band; ass:tangent-band.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:class-frontier]\label{def:class-frontier}
$K(underline_m, overline_m, underline_r) := K(underline_m, overline_m, underline_r) = sup_{nu in M(underline_m, overline_m, underline_r)} kappa_nu.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } underline_m, overline_m, underline_r, kappa_nu)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[def:frontier-duality-handle]\label{def:frontier-duality-handle}
$bounded-outcome dual frontier program := For v in (0,1), set q=v^2 and let mu_v be the unique solution in (q,v) of mu^4-2mu^3+2qmu^2-2q^2mu+q^2=0. Define rho(v)=((mu_v-q)(q-mu_v^2))/(4mu_v(1-mu_v)) and rho(1)=0. Let F(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r)={v in [underline_m,overline_m]: rho(v)>=underline_r}. For m_0,m_1 in F define Phi(m_0,m_1)={rho(m_0)m_1^2/m_0^3+rho(m_1)m_0^2/m_1^3}/[4(m_0+m_1)]. Then K(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r)=sup_{m_0,m_1 in F(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r)} Phi(m_0,m_1). If F is nonempty, the supremum is a maximum and is attained by taking the two arm marginals to be the corresponding three-point laws on {0,x_v,1}, x_v=(mu_v^2-2mu_v q+q)/(2mu_v(1-mu_v)), with weights p_x=(mu_v-q)/(x_v(1-x_v)), p_1=(q-x_v mu_v)/(1-x_v), and p_0=(x_v+q-(1+x_v)mu_v)/x_v. If F is empty, the regular band is empty and no attainment law is asserted.$ \quad (\text{inputs: } K(underline_m, overline_m, underline_r), J_{0,nu}(u_0), J_{1,nu}(u_1))
\end{definition}

\section{Main results}

\begin{lemma}[lem:path-existence]\label{lem:path-existence}
For every nu in M_tan and every direction u with 0 < J_nu(u) < infinity, there exists a bounded-support path h -> nu^(u,h) satisfying def:local-path-handle.
\end{lemma}
Fix nu in M_tan and u=(u_0,u_1) with 0<J_nu(u)<infinity. By lem:arm-score-program-solution, for each arm a there is a bounded score s_a=s_a^{u_a} satisfying int s_a dnu_a=0, int y s_a dnu_a=0, int y^2 s_a dnu_a=u_a, and int s_a^2 dnu_a=J_{a,nu}(u_a). Lemma lem:linear-tilt-path-valid then constructs, for all sufficiently small |h|, arm marginals dnu_a^h=(1+h s_a)dnu_a supported on [0,1], preserving the arm first moment exactly, perturbing the second moment as m_a(h)^2=m_a^2+h u_a, and satisfying KL(nu_a^h,nu_a)=(h^2/2)J_{a,nu}(u_a)+o(h^2).

Define nu^(u,0)=nu. For h not equal to zero, choose any coupling of the two tilted arm marginals, for instance the product coupling. This coupling is supported on [0,1]^2, so bounded support is preserved. The observed likelihood, the Neyman objective V_nu, the oracle allocation pi_nu_star, and the information calculation depend only on the arm marginals. Thus h -> nu^(u,h) is a bounded-support path through nu satisfying every clause of def:local-path-handle.
\par\smallskip\noindent \textit{Justification.} The lower bound must be carried by actual bounded-outcome alternatives, not by a formal score calculation on a class where second-moment perturbations may fail to exist. \textit{Gap.} Ma et al. (2021) provide an OPE Le Cam template, but not mean-preserving second-moment perturbations under a primitive rank condition that rules out degenerate bounded arm laws. \textit{Consumer.} This lemma supplies the concrete local alternatives needed to turn the information program into a valid bounded-outcome converse.

\begin{lemma}[lem:feasible-directions]\label{lem:feasible-directions}
For every nu in M_tan, the feasible-direction set U_nu is nonempty and 0 < kappa_nu < infinity.
\end{lemma}
Fix nu in M_tan and write p=pi_nu_star=m_1/(m_0+m_1). By ass:interior-second-moments, p lies in (0,1). By ass:arm-tangent-nondegeneracy and the definition of M_tan, r_{a,nu}>0 for a=0,1. Lemma lem:arm-score-program-solution gives J_{a,nu}(x)=x^2/r_{a,nu}. Hence J_nu(u)=p u_1^2/r_{1,nu}+(1-p)u_0^2/r_{0,nu}, a positive definite quadratic form on R^2.

The map dot_pi_nu(u) is a nonzero linear functional: for example, dot_pi_nu((0,1))=m_0/(2m_1(m_0+m_1)^2)>0. Therefore u=(0,1) has 0<J_nu(u)<infinity and dot_pi_nu(u) not equal to zero, so U_nu is nonempty.

Lemma lem:local-complexity-rayleigh gives the exact formula kappa_nu={r_{0,nu}m_1^2/m_0^3+r_{1,nu}m_0^2/m_1^3}/[4(m_0+m_1)]. Every term is finite and positive because 0<m_a<=1 under bounded outcomes and r_{a,nu}>0. Thus 0<kappa_nu<infinity.
\par\smallskip\noindent \textit{Justification.} The headline converse must quantify over a genuinely available local perturbation and a finite positive complexity constant. \textit{Gap.} The adaptive Neyman-allocation literature treats the oracle allocation as learnable but does not isolate the tangent nondegeneracy that makes the local information ratio well posed. \textit{Consumer.} This lemma licenses the epsilon-optimal direction used in the local minimax theorem and rules out empty-supremum or infinite-cost pathologies.

\begin{proposition}[prop:quadratic-loss-expansion]\label{prop:quadratic-loss-expansion}
For every nu in M_tan, g_nu(pi_nu_star + delta) = H_nu delta^2 + o(delta^2) as delta -> 0; along every path from lem:path-existence, pi_{nu^(u,h)}_star = pi_nu_star + h dot_pi_nu(u) + o(h) as h -> 0.

\begin{proposition}[prop:balanced-base-law]\label{prop:balanced-base-law}

\begin{openendedquestion}[oeq:closed-form-frontier]\label{oeq:closed-form-frontier}

This determines the exact class-level local lower-bound complexity over M(underline_m, overline_m, underline_r). This lower-bound complexity alone does not determine the sharp universal multiplicative constant of the full minimax regret without a matching constant-sharp upper bound.
\end{openendedquestion}
For any nu in the regular band, lem:local-complexity-rayleigh gives kappa_nu={r_{0,nu}m_1^2/m_0^3+r_{1,nu}m_0^2/m_1^3}/[4(m_0+m_1)]. Lemma lem:rho-envelope-closed-form says that, among all [0,1]-valued arm laws with root second moment v, the largest possible tangent strength r is rho(v), that rho is continuous on (0,1] with rho(1)=0, and that this value is attained by the three-point law specified in def:frontier-duality-handle. Consequently an arm with root second moment v can satisfy the regular-band tangent condition r>=underline_r if and only if rho(v)>=underline_r. This is exactly the feasible scalar set F(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r).

Because the coefficients multiplying r_{0,nu} and r_{1,nu} in the Rayleigh formula are positive, the supremum of kappa_nu over the regular band is obtained by maximizing each armwise r_a at fixed m_a. Therefore K(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r)=sup_{m_0,m_1 in F(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r)} Phi(m_0,m_1), where Phi is the function in def:frontier-duality-handle.

If F is empty, no arm marginal in the moment band can have r>=underline_r, so M(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r) is empty and no attainment law is asserted. If F is nonempty, F is closed in the compact interval [underline_m,overline_m] because rho is continuous, hence F^2 is compact. Since underline_m>0, Phi is continuous on F^2. The supremum is therefore a maximum. At a maximizer (m_0,m_1), choose for each arm the three-point extremal law from lem:rho-envelope-closed-form and couple the arms arbitrarily, for instance by product coupling. The resulting bounded-support nu lies in the regular band and attains the displayed value of K.

This solves the class-level local lower-bound frontier K over the closed regular band. Separately, thm:global-log-rate converts the strict-interior sup of kappa_nu into a matching-rate Omega(log T) minimax lower bound. These are aligned but not identical objects: K is the closed-band local frontier, while thm:global-log-rate uses sup_{nu in M^circ} kappa_nu to keep the local tilt inside the closed band. The constant-sharp upper bound matching K up to the universal multiplicative constant remains a separate question not implied by this lower-bound frontier alone.

(2024) expose upper-bound parameter dependence, but no paper solves the class-level lower-bound extremal program that would identify the exact unavoidable constant. \textit{Consumer.} A closed-form class frontier would let later work test whether clipping and lower-bound constants in existing adaptive designs are artifacts or information-theoretic necessities.

\begin{lemma}[lem:arm-score-program-solution]\label{lem:arm-score-program-solution}
For nu in M_tan and a in {0,1}, let e_a be the L2(nu_a) residual of y^2 after projection on span{1,y}. Then r_{a,nu}=int e_a^2 dnu_a>0. For every x in R, J_{a,nu}(x)=x^2/r_{a,nu}; when x is nonzero the minimizer is s_a^x=x e_a/r_{a,nu}, and for x=0 the minimizer is s_a^0=0. These scores are bounded on [0,1].
\end{lemma}
Work in the Hilbert space L2(nu_a). Let L=span{1,y}, a finite-dimensional closed subspace. By def:arm-tangent-strengths, r_{a,nu}=inf_{ell in L} ||y^2-ell||_2^2. Since nu lies in M_tan, this value is positive. Let e_a=y^2-Proj_L(y^2). Then e_a is orthogonal to 1 and y, and ||e_a||_2^2=r_{a,nu}. If s is feasible for J_{a,nu}(x), then int s dnu_a=0 and int y s dnu_a=0, so x=int y^2 s dnu_a=int e_a s dnu_a. Cauchy-Schwarz gives x^2<=||s||_2^2 r_{a,nu}, hence every feasible score has cost at least x^2/r_{a,nu}. The score s_a^x=x e_a/r_{a,nu} satisfies the mass and first-moment constraints, has int y^2 s_a^x dnu_a=x, and has squared norm x^2/r_{a,nu}; for x=0 this is the zero score. Thus J_{a,nu}(x)=x^2/r_{a,nu}. Since y is supported on [0,1] and e_a is a quadratic polynomial minus an affine polynomial with finite coefficients, e_a and s_a^x are bounded on [0,1].

\begin{lemma}[lem:linear-tilt-path-valid]\label{lem:linear-tilt-path-valid}
For nu in M_tan and every u with 0<J_nu(u)<infinity, the scores from lem:arm-score-program-solution define a linear-tilt path dnu_a^h=(1+h s_a^{u_a})dnu_a for small |h|. This path has bounded support, preserves each arm mean exactly, perturbs the arm second moment as m_a(h)^2=m_a^2+h u_a, and satisfies KL(nu_a^h,nu_a)=(h^2/2)J_{a,nu}(u_a)+o(h^2).
\end{lemma}
Let s_a=s_a^{u_a} be the bounded score supplied by lem:arm-score-program-solution. Choose eta>0 such that |h| ||s_a||_infty<=1/2 for both arms whenever |h|<=eta. Then 1+h s_a is nonnegative and integrates to one because int s_a dnu_a=0, so dnu_a^h=(1+h s_a)dnu_a is a probability law supported on [0,1]. Its first moment is int y dnu_a+h int y s_a dnu_a=int y dnu_a. Its second moment is int y^2 dnu_a+h int y^2 s_a dnu_a=m_a^2+h u_a. Boundedness of s_a gives the uniform expansion (1+h s_a)log(1+h s_a)=h s_a+(h^2/2)s_a^2+o(h^2), and the linear term integrates to zero. Therefore KL(nu_a^h,nu_a)=(h^2/2)int s_a^2 dnu_a+o(h^2)=(h^2/2)J_{a,nu}(u_a)+o(h^2).

\begin{lemma}[lem:neyman-gap-identity]\label{lem:neyman-gap-identity}
For any law with m_0,m_1>0, let S=m_0+m_1 and p=pi_nu_star=m_1/S. Then for every pi in (0,1), V_nu(pi)-V_nu(p)=S^2(pi-p)^2/[pi(1-pi)].
\end{lemma}
Since p=m_1/S, m_1=Sp and m_0=S(1-p). Hence V_nu(pi)=S^2 p^2/pi+S^2(1-p)^2/(1-pi), while V_nu(p)=S^2 p+S^2(1-p)=S^2. Thus V_nu(pi)-V_nu(p)=S^2{p^2/pi+(1-p)^2/(1-pi)-1}. Putting the terms over pi(1-pi), the numerator is p^2(1-pi)+(1-p)^2 pi-pi(1-pi)=p^2-2p pi+pi^2=(pi-p)^2. This proves the identity.

\begin{lemma}[lem:bayes-cramer-rao-predictable]\label{lem:bayes-cramer-rao-predictable}
Let h have a continuously differentiable prior density q on an interval [a,b], with q(a)=q(b)=0 and I_q=int (q'(h)^2/q(h))dh<infinity. Let Z have law P_h differentiable in quadratic mean with score S_h and Fisher information I(h)=E_h S_h^2. For any square-integrable estimator delta(Z) of a differentiable scalar psi(h), E_q E_h[(delta(Z)-psi(h))^2] >= (int psi'(h)q(h)dh)^2 / (I_q + int I(h)q(h)dh). The same bound holds when Z is the history of a predictable sequential experiment, with I(h) equal to the Fisher information of the joint likelihood through that history.
\end{lemma}
Let E denote expectation under the joint density q(h)dP_h(z). The joint score for h is S_h(Z)+q'(h)/q(h). Since delta(Z) does not depend on h and q vanishes at the endpoints, integration by parts gives E[(delta(Z)-psi(h))(S_h(Z)+q'(h)/q(h))]=int psi'(h)q(h)dh. Cauchy-Schwarz bounds the square of this identity by E[(delta-psi)^2] E[(S_h+q'/q)^2]. The cross term is zero because E_h S_h=0 for each h, so the second factor is int I(h)q(h)dh+I_q. This proves the displayed inequality. In a predictable sequential experiment, the likelihood factorizes into conditional likelihoods. Differentiating the product gives an additive martingale score, and conditional orthogonality makes the Fisher information the sum of the conditional Fisher informations through the observed history. Applying the same integration-by-parts argument to this joint likelihood proves the sequential version.

\begin{lemma}[lem:local-neighborhood-cumulative-risk]\label{lem:local-neighborhood-cumulative-risk}
Fix nu in M_tan and u in U_nu. For the linear-tilt path h -> nu^(u,h), with S=m_0+m_1, there exists bar_eta>0 such that for every eta in (0,bar_eta] there is T_0(eta)<infinity such that every Alg in A_T satisfies sup_{|h|<=eta} mathfrak_R_T(Alg,nu^(u,h)) >= c_0 S^2 dot_pi_nu(u)^2 J_nu(u)^{-1} log T for all T>=T_0(eta), where c_0>0 is a universal numerical constant.
\end{lemma}
Write p_h=pi_{nu^(u,h)}_star, S_h=m_0(h)+m_1(h), J=J_nu(u), d=dot_pi_nu(u), and S=m_0+m_1. Since u lies in U_nu, J>0 and d is nonzero. By prop:quadratic-loss-expansion and the exact moment formulas for the linear tilt, there is bar_eta>0 such that for every |h|<=bar_eta, the following four bounds hold simultaneously: p'_h has the same sign as d and |p'_h|>=|d|/2; S_h^2>=S^2/2; p_h stays in a compact subinterval of (0,1); and |p_h I_1(h)+(1-p_h)I_0(h)-J|<=J/4, where I_a(h)=int s_a(y)^2/(1+h s_a(y)) dnu_a(y) is the armwise Fisher information of the tilted marginal. By shrinking bar_eta if necessary, 1+h s_a is bounded away from zero for both arms on this interval. Lemma lem:neyman-gap-identity gives, for every pi in (0,1),

g_{nu^(u,h)}(pi)=S_h^2(pi-p_h)^2/[pi(1-pi)].

Since pi(1-pi)<=1/4, we have g_{nu^(u,h)}(pi)>=4S_h^2(pi-p_h)^2>=2S^2(pi-p_h)^2 for every |h|<=bar_eta.

Now fix any eta in (0,bar_eta] and any adaptive design Alg. Choose a C^1 prior density q_eta on [-eta,eta] with q_eta(-eta)=q_eta(eta)=0 and prior Fisher information I_q=int (q_eta')^2/q_eta=C_q eta^{-2}. Define

b_t=int E_h[(pi_t-p_h)^2]q_eta(h)dh,    B_T=sum_{t=1}^T b_t,

and let R_T^B=int E_h sum_{t=1}^T g_{nu^(u,h)}(pi_t) q_eta(h)dh be the Bayes-average cumulative regret. The quadratic inequality above gives R_T^B>=2S^2 B_T.

For the history before time t, predictability and Bernoulli randomization give the conditional Fisher information under h as

sum_{s<t} E_h[pi_s I_1(h)+(1-pi_s)I_0(h)].

Decompose the summand as p_h I_1(h)+(1-p_h)I_0(h)+(pi_s-p_h)(I_1(h)-I_0(h)). The first term is at most 5J/4 on [-eta,eta]. The second term is bounded in absolute value by L |pi_s-p_h| for a finite path constant L, because the scores are bounded and 1+h s_a is bounded away from zero. Averaging over q_eta and applying Cauchy-Schwarz gives integrated Fisher information before t at most (5J/4)t+L sqrt(t B_{t-1}). Applying lem:bayes-cramer-rao-predictable to the estimator delta=pi_t of psi(h)=p_h based on H_{t-1}, and using int |p'_h| q_eta(h)dh>=|d|/2 with constant sign, yields

b_t >= (d^2/4)/(C_q eta^{-2}+(5J/4)t+L sqrt(t B_{t-1})).

Let A>0 be a sufficiently small numerical constant. If R_T^B>=A S^2 d^2 J^{-1} log T, then the desired lower bound follows because sup_{|h|<=eta} mathfrak_R_T(Alg,nu^(u,h)) is at least the Bayes average. Otherwise B_T<=A d^2 J^{-1}log T/2. Set t_*=ceil(C_* log T), where C_* is a finite constant chosen for the fixed local path so that the feedback term satisfies L sqrt(t A d^2 J^{-1}log T/2) <= (J/2)t for all t in [t_*,T]. Since eta is fixed, choose T_0(eta) large enough that also C_q eta^{-2}<=(J/4)t for every t>=t_* and T>=T_0(eta). Hence, for T>=T_0(eta) and all t in [t_*,T], the denominator in the lower bound for b_t is at most 2Jt, and therefore b_t>=d^2/(8Jt).

Consequently

\begin{lemma}[lem:band-continuity-for-linear-tilts]\label{lem:band-continuity-for-linear-tilts}

\end{lemma}
Along the linear-tilt path, every arm moment up to order four is a continuous function of h, because it is an integral of a bounded polynomial against (1+h s_a)dnu_a. In particular m_a(h)^2=m_a^2+h u_a is continuous, and m_a(h) is continuous for small h because m_a>0. The tangent strength r_{a,nu^(u,h)} is the minimum over b=(b_0,b_1) of int (y^2-b_0-b_1y)^2 dnu_a^h. At h=0 this minimum is positive, so the Gram matrix of (1,y) is nonsingular; by continuity of moments it remains nonsingular for small h, and the projection coefficients and minimized residual are continuous functions of moments through order four. Since nu is strict interior, m_a(0) has positive distance from underline_m and overline_m, and r_{a,nu} has positive distance above underline_r. Continuity gives eta>0 such that underline_m<=m_a(h)<=overline_m and r_{a,nu^(u,h)}>=underline_r for both arms and all |h|<=eta. Bounded support is preserved by the tilt, so nu^(u,h) belongs to M(underline_m,overline_m,underline_r).

\begin{lemma}[lem:local-complexity-rayleigh]\label{lem:local-complexity-rayleigh}
For every nu in M_tan, with S=m_0+m_1, p=m_1/S, and r_a=r_{a,nu}, J_nu(u)=p u_1^2/r_1+(1-p)u_0^2/r_0 and kappa_nu={r_0 m_1^2/m_0^3+r_1 m_0^2/m_1^3}/[4S].
\end{lemma}
The expression for J_nu follows immediately from lem:arm-score-program-solution and def:local-information. Thus J_nu is the positive definite quadratic form p u_1^2/r_1+(1-p)u_0^2/r_0. Also dot_pi_nu(u)=[(m_0/m_1)u_1-(m_1/m_0)u_0]/(2S^2).

Here alpha=m_0/m_1, beta=-m_1/m_0, a=p/r_1=m_1/(S r_1), and b=(1-p)/r_0=m_0/(S r_0). Therefore sup_u S^2 dot_pi_nu(u)^2/J_nu(u)=1/(4S^2){(m_0/m_1)^2 S r_1/m_1+(m_1/m_0)^2 S r_0/m_0}. Simplifying gives kappa_nu={r_0 m_1^2/m_0^3+r_1 m_0^2/m_1^3}/[4S].

\begin{lemma}[lem:rho-envelope-closed-form]\label{lem:rho-envelope-closed-form}
For v in (0,1), let q=v^2. Among all laws mu on [0,1] with int y^2 dmu=q, the supremum of r(mu)=inf_{b_0,b_1} int (y^2-b_0-b_1 y)^2 dmu is rho(v)=((mu_v-q)(q-mu_v^2))/(4mu_v(1-mu_v)), where mu_v in (q,v) is the unique root of mu^4-2mu^3+2qmu^2-2q^2mu+q^2=0. The supremum is attained by the three-point law on {0,x_v,1} with the weights displayed in def:frontier-duality-handle; rho extends continuously to v=1 by rho(1)=0.
\end{lemma}
Let Y be [0,1]-valued, let mu=E Y, q=E Y^2, a=E Y^3, and b=E Y^4. Necessarily q<=mu<=sqrt(q). If q is 0 or 1 the law is degenerate at 0 or 1 and r=0; hence take q in (0,1) and mu in (q,sqrt(q)). The residual variance after projecting Y^2 on span{1,Y} is r=(b-q^2)-(a-mu q)^2/(q-mu^2).

For fixed mu and q, the Hausdorff localizing inequality int Y(1-Y)(c_0+c_1Y)^2 dP>=0 gives the positive-semidefinite matrix [[mu-q,q-a],[q-a,a-b]]. Hence b<=a-(q-a)^2/(mu-q). Substituting this upper bound into the display for r gives r<=a-(q-a)^2/(mu-q)-q^2-(a-mu q)^2/(q-mu^2). The right side is a concave quadratic function of a. Its maximizer is a_*=(mu^3-mu^2 q+2mu q^2-mu q-q^2)/(2mu(mu-1)), and the maximum value is R(mu,q)=((mu-q)(q-mu^2))/(4mu(1-mu)).

The bound is sharp. Let x=(mu^2-2mu q+q)/(2mu(1-mu)) and assign masses p_x=(mu-q)/(x(1-x)), p_1=(q-x mu)/(1-x), and p_0=(x+q-(1+x)mu)/x to x,1,0. For mu in (q,sqrt(q)), these quantities are nonnegative and sum to one, and direct substitution gives E Y=mu and E Y^2=q. The same substitution gives E Y^3=a_* and saturates b=a-(q-a)^2/(mu-q), so r=R(mu,q).

It remains to maximize over mu. For q=v^2, R(mu,q) vanishes at the endpoints mu=q and mu=v and is positive inside. Differentiation gives dR/dmu=[mu^4-2mu^3+2qmu^2-2q^2mu+q^2]/[4mu^2(1-mu)^2]. The Sturm sign calculation for this quartic on (q,v) gives exactly one zero, denoted mu_v in def:frontier-duality-handle; the derivative is positive to the left of that zero and negative to the right. Hence the unique maximizer is mu_v, the envelope is rho(v)=R(mu_v,q), and the displayed three-point law with mu=mu_v attains it. The implicit-function theorem on the unique interior maximizer gives continuity of rho on (0,1). As v tends to 1, the constraint E Y^2=v^2 forces Y to converge to 1 in L2, so the residual variance r is at most Var(Y^2), which tends to zero; thus rho(v)->0=rho(1).
