\begin{abstract}
This note studies the local partial-identification problem for the policy-regret target
$\theta_{\pi,\pi_0}=\E[Y(\pi_0)-Y(\pi)]$ when the observed law is $P$ for
$O=(X,A,Y)$, $A$ is binary, $Y$ is bounded, and the candidate policy class is finite.
For a policy score $W_\pi$ and its covariate projection
$M_\pi(X)=\E[W_\pi\mid X]$, the estimand-defining bounding functional is
\[
  \theta^A_L(P,\pi)=\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)-B_A(P,\pi),
  \qquad
  \theta^A_U(P,\pi)=\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+B_A(P,\pi),
\]
where
\[
  B_C=\kappa\|W_\pi\|_2,\qquad
  B_M=b(\Gamma)\inf_{c\in\R}\E|W_\pi-c|,\qquad
  B_S=\delta\|M_\pi\|_2.
\]
The main result proves that these are sharp bounds for the stated local support-function
model and confirms the positive all-pairs scalar-calibration frontier: a single scalar
translation among the three sensitivity languages exists on an active finite policy class
if and only if the finite diagnostic matrix of the three penalties has positive rank one.
The note also refutes the proposed ``open set with exactly equal CH norms'' witness as
stated, because exact equality of two continuously varying norms is not an open property
without an additional normalization, and gives the nearest correct finite-support witness
with an open non-calibration neighborhood.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Offline policy learning often reports robustness to hidden confounding through one
scalar sensitivity parameter.  A partial-$R^2$ robustness value, a marginal-sensitivity
odds-ratio parameter, and a covariate-shift radius are all interpretable within their
own models, but they constrain different local perturbations of the latent full-data
law.  The causal question in this note is therefore not whether one language is
``more conservative'' in general; it is whether one can translate the scalar attached
to one language into a scalar attached to another language without changing robust
policy-regret comparisons over a finite policy class.

Known sensitivity analyses supply the component geometries.  Partial-$R^2$ calibration
uses an $L^2$ omitted-variable geometry; marginal sensitivity uses a bounded
odds-ratio, locally represented here by a mean-zero $L^\infty$ perturbation; and
external-validity or covariate-shift analyses use covariate-only tilts.  Before making
cross-language comparisons, these geometries must be reduced to support functions of
the same policy score.  This note performs that reduction and then solves the finite
all-pairs calibration problem.

The new contribution is the exact diagnostic.  For each active policy comparison, the
observable score penalties are
\[
  n_C(\pi)=\|W_\pi\|_2,\qquad
  n_M(\pi)=\inf_{c\in\R}\E|W_\pi-c|,\qquad
  n_S(\pi)=\|\E[W_\pi\mid X]\|_2.
\]
A policy-invariant scalar bridge among the three sensitivity languages exists exactly
when the matrix with rows $(n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi))$ has all cross-language
$2\times2$ minors equal to zero and all active entries positive.  This condition is
useful because it is a finite observable diagnostic: if it fails, separate robustness
curves are mathematically necessary, except for deliberately policy-specific
calibrations that no longer preserve a common sensitivity scale.
Throughout the conjecture statements below, $D_1(W)$ denotes
$\inf_{c\in\R}\E|W-c|$.

\paragraph{Motivating conjecture(s).}
\begin{conjecture}[Conjecture 1: observable scalar-calibration threshold (NOT YET PROVED)]\label{conj:calibration-frontier}
Under Assumptions \ref{ass:bounded-overlap}--\ref{ass:active-domain}, a non-degenerate three-language scalar bridge among $B_C$, $B_M$, and $B_S$ on the active finite class $\Pi_\ast$ exists on an interval of sensitivity values if and only if the observable bridge-defect threshold is zero: $\tau_{\Pi_\ast}=0$, with $n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi)>0$ for every $\pi\in\Pi_\ast$.  Equivalently, $Q_{\Pi_\ast}$ lies on the positive common-scale frontier, so there are positive constants $\alpha_C,\alpha_M,\alpha_S$ and positive policy weights $\rho(\pi)$ such that
\[
  \bigl(n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi)\bigr)
  = \rho(\pi)(\alpha_C,\alpha_M,\alpha_S)
  \qquad\text{for every }\pi\in\Pi_\ast.
\]
Assumption \ref{ass:rank-one} is a sufficient primitive condition for this positive common-scale frontier.  Conversely, under Assumption \ref{ass:two-score}, $\tau_{\Pi_\ast}>0$ and no scalar maps $\kappa\mapsto\Gamma(\kappa)$ and $\kappa\mapsto\delta(\kappa)$ can satisfy
\[
  B_C(\pi;\kappa)=B_M(\pi;\Gamma(\kappa))=B_S(\pi;\delta(\kappa))
\]
for all $\pi\in\Pi_\ast$ on any non-degenerate interval with $\kappa>0$.  If the positive common-scale condition holds, then for every nominal regret vector $(\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0})_{\pi\in\Pi_\ast}$ the robust-regret endpoint vectors $(R_C(\pi))_{\pi\in\Pi_\ast}$, $(R_M(\pi))_{\pi\in\Pi_\ast}$, and $(R_S(\pi))_{\pi\in\Pi_\ast}$ agree up to the same positive radius normalization, so the local choice sets $\mathcal A_C(\kappa)$, $\mathcal A_M(\Gamma(\kappa))$, and $\mathcal A_S(\delta(\kappa))$ coincide.  Moreover, whenever one residual is nonzero for $\pi_1,\pi_2$, there is an open interval of nominal regret differences $\theta^0_{\pi_1,\pi_0}-\theta^0_{\pi_2,\pi_0}$ for which the CH-normalized ranking and at least one of the MSM robust-welfare ranking induced by \citet{JinXuZhengZhou2025} or the covariate-shift policy-choice ranking induced by \citet{AdjahoChristensen2025} choose different unique policies.
\end{conjecture}

\begin{conjecture}[Conjecture 2: supporting two-policy open-set penalty separation witness (NOT YET PROVED)]\label{conj:open-set-witness}
Under Assumptions \ref{ass:bounded-overlap}--\ref{ass:active-domain}, there is an open set of non-degenerate binary-action observable laws $P$ and two active policies $\pi_1,\pi_2\in\Pi_\ast$ for which
\[
  \|W_{\pi_1}\|_2=\|W_{\pi_2}\|_2
  \quad\text{but}\quad
  D_1(W_{\pi_1})\ne D_1(W_{\pi_2})
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  \|M_{\pi_1}\|_2\ne\|M_{\pi_2}\|_2.
\]
For those laws, CH partial-$R^2$ robustness ties the two penalty terms at every $\kappa$, while the MSM or covariate-shift penalty terms strictly separate at every positive calibrated radius.  If, in addition, Assumption \ref{ass:nominal-tie} holds, then the same witness gives a robust-regret ranking separation; without this nominal-regret equality, the conjecture is only a penalty-level sharpness witness and the ranking claim is supplied by Conjecture \ref{conj:calibration-frontier}.
\end{conjecture}

\section{Related work and positioning}
\cite{Manski1990} frames the partial-identification viewpoint used here: under incomplete
identifying restrictions, the estimand is bracketed by a set or interval rather than
replaced by a point estimand.  \cite{BalkePearl1997} show how finite instrumental-variable
models yield sharp bounds by explicit linear programming; this note uses the same
support-function logic, but the feasible sets are local norm balls for policy-score
perturbations rather than finite response-function types.  \cite{CinelliHazlett2020}
develop partial-$R^2$ omitted-variable sensitivity; their $L^2$ geometry supplies the
CH side of the comparison, but they do not study finite policy-ranking calibration.

\cite{ZhaoSmallBhattacharya2019} and \cite{KallusZhou2021} use odds-ratio sensitivity
for inverse-propensity weighting and policy learning, respectively; their models motivate
the bounded local perturbation language whose support norm is the centered absolute
deviation $D_1$.  \cite{YadlowskyNamkoongBasuDuchiTian2022} and \cite{DornGuoKallus2025}
develop sharp and doubly robust sensitivity bounds under unmeasured confounding; they
motivate why the support functions should be treated as partial-identification bounds
rather than as heuristic penalties.  \cite{JinXuZhengZhou2025} gives robust welfare
criteria for policy learning under marginal sensitivity, making it a direct downstream
use case for knowing whether a CH-style scalar can reproduce MSM policy rankings.

\cite{DiegertMastenPoirier2025} compare omitted-variable sensitivity parameters
axiomatically; that work is close in spirit, but it compares scalar parameters rather
than finite policy-regret support functions.  \cite{MastenPoirierRen2025} give a
general relaxation framework for unconfoundedness; this note is narrower because it
fixes the local CH, MSM, and covariate-shift support sets and asks only when their
policy rankings can be scalar-calibrated.  \cite{SorianoBenMichaelBickelFellerPimentel2023} and \cite{HuangPimentel2025} study
interpretable and variance-based sensitivity analyses for weighting estimators; they
are close because they connect weighting sensitivity to scalar calibration, but their
object is an estimator interval rather than an all-pairs finite policy-regret frontier.
\cite{AdjahoChristensen2025} studies externally valid policy choice under target
population shifts, and \cite{ChristensenConnault2023} studies counterfactual robustness
under latent distributional ambiguity; they position the covariate-shift side of this
note.  The closest in-project partial-identification material concerns bounded-outcome
and Manski-style potential-outcome intervals.  Those tools share the interval logic of
this note, but the current theorem is a local support-function calibration result for
an arbitrary finite collection of policy scores, so its primitive objects are norms,
conditional projections, and finite diagnostic matrices.

\section{Formal setup}
Let $O=(X,A,Y)\sim P$ be the observed data vector.  The action is binary,
$A\in\{0,1\}$, the outcome is bounded, $Y\in[\ell,u]$ almost surely, and the
known nominal propensity $e_0(a\mid X)$ is bounded away from $0$ and $1$.  The
policy class $\Pi$ is finite, each policy is a measurable rule
$\pi:\mathcal X\to\{0,1\}$, and $\pi_0\in\Pi$ is the status-quo policy.

When the nominal outcome regressions $m_a(x)=\E[Y\mid A=a,X=x]$ are identified from
$P$, the observable policy-value score used by the diagnostic is
\[
  \widetilde W_\pi(O)
  =
  m_{\pi_0(X)}(X)-m_{\pi(X)}(X)
  + {\one\{A=\pi_0(X)\}\over e_0(A\mid X)}\{Y-m_A(X)\}
  - {\one\{A=\pi(X)\}\over e_0(A\mid X)}\{Y-m_A(X)\},
  \qquad
  W_\pi=\widetilde W_\pi-\E[\widetilde W_\pi].
\]
The later derivation only requires that this centered bounded score is the local
derivative of the regret target; the display records the observable specialization
used for the policy-score calibration diagnostic.

For each $\pi\in\Pi$, let $\theta_{\pi,\pi_0}$ denote the latent full-data policy
regret contrast
\[
  \theta_{\pi,\pi_0}=\E_{\mathcal L}\{Y(\pi_0)-Y(\pi)\},
\]
where $\mathcal L$ ranges over full-data laws compatible with the observed law
$P$ and the maintained local sensitivity restriction.  Let
$\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)$ be the nominal compatible value at the reference law.
The local model assumes that compatible perturbation paths have first-order
regret score $W_\pi\in L^2_0(P)\cap L^\infty(P)$:
\[
  \theta_{\pi,\pi_0}(\varepsilon)-\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)
  = \varepsilon\,\E[hW_\pi]+o(\varepsilon)
\]
for a mean-zero latent perturbation score $h$.  For covariate shifts define
\[
  M_\pi(X)=\E[W_\pi\mid X].
\]
Since $\E W_\pi=0$, also $\E M_\pi(X)=0$.

The three local ambiguity sets are
\[
  \mathcal H_C(\kappa)=\{h\in L^2(P):\E h=0,\ \E h^2\le \kappa^2\},
\]
\[
  \mathcal H_M(\Gamma)=\{h\in L^\infty(P):\E h=0,\ \|h\|_\infty\le b(\Gamma)\},
  \qquad b(1)=0,\quad b(\Gamma)>0\text{ for }\Gamma>1,
\]
and
\[
  \mathcal G_S(\delta)=\{g\in L^2(P_X):\E g(X)=0,\ \E g(X)^2\le \delta^2\}.
\]
The local identified set for a single policy comparison is
\[
  \Theta_C(P;\pi,\kappa)
  =\{\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+\E[hW_\pi]:h\in\mathcal H_C(\kappa)\},
\]
\[
  \Theta_M(P;\pi,\Gamma)
  =\{\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+\E[hW_\pi]:h\in\mathcal H_M(\Gamma)\},
\]
and
\[
  \Theta_S(P;\pi,\delta)
  =\{\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+\E[g(X)W_\pi]:g\in\mathcal G_S(\delta)\}.
\]
The notation $\Theta_I(P)$ in this note refers to one of these three local identified
sets, with the language and radius clear from context.

For an active finite set $\Pi_\ast\subseteq\Pi\setminus\{\pi_0\}$ define
\[
  D_1(W)=\inf_{c\in\R}\E|W-c|,
  \qquad
  n_C(\pi)=\|W_\pi\|_2,\quad
  n_M(\pi)=D_1(W_\pi),\quad
  n_S(\pi)=\|M_\pi\|_2.
\]
The diagnostic matrix is
\[
  Q_{\Pi_\ast}=\bigl(n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi)\bigr)_{\pi\in\Pi_\ast},
\]
and for two languages $A,B\in\{C,M,S\}$ and two policies $\pi,\pi'$ the all-pairs
calibration residual is
\[
  \Delta_{AB}(\pi,\pi')
  =n_A(\pi)n_B(\pi')-n_A(\pi')n_B(\pi).
\]
The positive common-scale frontier is the event that all these residuals vanish and
$n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi)$ are strictly positive for every active policy.
Equivalently, the observable bridge-defect threshold
\[
  \tau_{\Pi_\ast}
  =
  \max_{A<B}\max_{\pi,\pi'\in\Pi_\ast}|\Delta_{AB}(\pi,\pi')|
\]
is zero, with strictly positive active entries.

\[
  \theta_{\pi,\pi_0}=\E[Y(\pi_0)-Y(\pi)]

For each candidate policy $\pi$, the local policy score $W_{\pi}$ is the centered, bounded observed score that represents the first-order perturbation of $\theta_{\pi,\pi_0}$ under compatible latent confounding or target-population tilts.  The restriction $\mathcal C_{\kappa}$ is a Cinelli--Hazlett partial-$R^2$ budget represented locally as an $L^2(P)$ ellipsoid for mean-zero latent perturbations $h$: $\E h=0$ and $\E h^2\le \kappa^2$.  The restriction $\mathcal M_{\Gamma}$ is the marginal-sensitivity odds-ratio budget represented locally by mean-zero perturbations $h$ with $\|h\|_{\infty}\le b(\Gamma)$, where $b(1)=0$ and $b(\Gamma)$ is increasing. The bounding functional $B_{\mathfrak A}(\pi)$ is the support-function regret penalty for ambiguity language $\mathfrak A\in\{\mathcal C_{\kappa},\mathcal M_{\Gamma},\mathcal S_{\delta}\}$; the proposed kernel concerns when there is, or is not, a positive scalar calibration among these $B_{\mathfrak A}$ values that preserves regret rankings over the active nonzero policy comparisons in $\Pi$.

\section{Assumptions}
\begin{assumption}[Bounded overlap and bounded outcomes]\label{ass:bounded-overlap}
The observed law $P$ satisfies $Y\in[\ell,u]$ almost surely and
$e_0(a\mid X)\in[\underline e,1-\underline e]$ for some $\underline e>0$ and
both $a\in\{0,1\}$.  All conditional expectations used below are taken on
events with positive $P$-probability, and all score functions are identified up
to $P$-almost-sure equality.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Finite policy class and local differentiability]\label{ass:finite-local}
The class $\Pi$ is finite, contains $\pi_0$, and for every $\pi\in\Pi$ the regret
contrast $\theta_{\pi,\pi_0}$ is locally differentiable along compatible latent
perturbation paths with centered derivative score
$W_\pi\in L^2_0(P)\cap L^\infty(P)$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Support-function sensitivity budgets]\label{ass:support-budgets}
The CH, MSM, and covariate-shift ambiguity languages are represented locally by
the support sets in the Formal setup section: $\E h=0$, $\E h^2\le\kappa^2$ for
$\mathcal C_{\kappa}$; $\E h=0$, $\|h\|_{\infty}\le b(\Gamma)$ for
$\mathcal M_{\Gamma}$; and $\E g(X)=0$, $\E g(X)^2\le\delta^2$ for
$\mathcal S_{\delta}$.  The calibration scale $b$ is continuous, strictly
increasing, satisfies $b(1)=0$, and has range $[0,\infty)$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Active positive bridge domain]\label{ass:active-domain}
The three-language bridge is evaluated on a nonempty active subset
$\Pi_\ast\subseteq\Pi\setminus\{\pi_0\}$ satisfying
$n_C(\pi)>0$, $n_M(\pi)>0$, and $n_S(\pi)>0$ for every $\pi\in\Pi_\ast$.
A policy with $n_A(\pi)=0$ for some bridged language is not part of a
non-degenerate three-language bridge unless all bridged penalties are zero for
that policy, in which case it is an uninformative tie and is omitted from
$\Pi_\ast$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Rank-one compatibility frontier]\label{ass:rank-one}
There exists a nonzero centered score $W_0\in L^2_0(P)\cap L^{\infty}(P)$ and
constants $c_{\pi}\ne0$ such that $W_{\pi}=c_{\pi}W_0$ for every
$\pi\in\Pi_\ast$.  For the three-language bridge, additionally
$M_{\pi}(X)=d_{\pi}M_0(X)$ for a nonzero centered covariate score $M_0$ and
constants $d_{\pi}\ne0$ with $|d_{\pi}|$ proportional to $|c_{\pi}|$ on
$\Pi_\ast$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Two-score generic witness]\label{ass:two-score}
There exist $\pi_1,\pi_2\in\Pi_\ast$ such that $W_{\pi_1}$ and $W_{\pi_2}$ are
linearly independent in $L^2_0(P)$, have non-degenerate distributions, and have
strictly positive relevant penalties $n_A(\pi_j)>0$ for the languages $A$ being
compared.  At least one all-pairs calibration residual is nonzero:
\[
  \Delta_{CM}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  \Delta_{CS}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  \Delta_{MS}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0.
\]
This cross-product formulation is the locked convention for avoiding undefined
norm ratios.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Nominal-regret tie for the ranking witness]\label{ass:nominal-tie}
For the two witness policies in the motivating Conjecture 2, the nominal regrets
are equal:
\[
  \theta^0_{\pi_1,\pi_0}=\theta^0_{\pi_2,\pi_0}.
\]
This assumption is used only for the optional robust-regret ranking conclusion;
the penalty-level separation does not require it.
\end{assumption}

\section{Estimand-defining functional}
For each policy $\pi$ and ambiguity language $A\in\{C,M,S\}$, define the support
penalty
\[
  B_C(P,\pi;\kappa)=\kappa\,\|W_\pi\|_2,
\]
\[
  B_M(P,\pi;\Gamma)=b(\Gamma)\,D_1(W_\pi),
  \qquad
  D_1(W_\pi)=\inf_{c\in\R}\E|W_\pi-c|,
\]
and
\[
  B_S(P,\pi;\delta)=\delta\,\|M_\pi\|_2,
  \qquad M_\pi(X)=\E[W_\pi\mid X].
\]
The local lower and upper endpoints are
\[
  \theta^A_L(P,\pi;t_A)=\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)-B_A(P,\pi;t_A),
  \qquad
  \theta^A_U(P,\pi;t_A)=\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+B_A(P,\pi;t_A),
\]
where $t_C=\kappa$, $t_M=\Gamma$, and $t_S=\delta$.  The robust-regret upper
endpoint used for policy ranking is
\[
  R_A(P,\pi;t_A)=\theta^A_U(P,\pi;t_A).
\]

These endpoints are computable from observed-population inputs once the local scores
$W_\pi$ have been constructed from the nominal policy-regret model.  The required
operators are the $L^2(P)$ norm, the conditional expectation $M_\pi(X)=\E[W_\pi\mid X]$,
and the one-dimensional convex minimization defining $D_1$.  In a finite support
law with $m$ support points, $D_1(W_\pi)$ is computed by sorting the $m$ values of
$W_\pi$ and choosing any weighted median; the complexity is $O(m\log m)$ per policy.
In a general law, any median of $W_\pi$ minimizes $c\mapsto\E|W_\pi-c|$, so the
functional is the expected absolute deviation from a median.

A scalar bridge from language $A$ to language $B$ on $\Pi_\ast$ is a positive
parameter map $t_B=\varphi_{BA}(t_A)$, independent of $\pi$, such that
\[
  B_A(P,\pi;t_A)=B_B(P,\pi;\varphi_{BA}(t_A))
  \quad\text{for every }\pi\in\Pi_\ast
\]
on a non-degenerate interval of positive $t_A$ values.  A reference-policy
calibration from $A$ to $B$ at $\pi_1$ instead imposes equality only at $\pi_1$:
if $s_A n_A(\pi)$ and $s_B n_B(\pi)$ are the two penalties, it sets
$s_B=s_A n_A(\pi_1)/n_B(\pi_1)$.

\paragraph{Verdict on the motivating conjectures.}
Conjecture 1 is confirmed for the local support-function model: the positive
common-scale frontier is necessary and sufficient for a non-degenerate scalar bridge,
and any nonzero residual gives a two-policy reference-calibration ranking witness after
choosing nominal regret differences in an explicit open interval.  Conjecture 2 is
refuted as written: exact equality $\|W_{\pi_1}\|_2=\|W_{\pi_2}\|_2$ is not an open
condition for continuously varying laws unless it is imposed by an additional
normalization.  The nearest correct statement is a finite-support equality witness at
one law, together with an open neighborhood on which the nonzero calibration residual
and the ranking-separation conclusion persist.

\section{Target causal estimand}
The causal target for a candidate policy $\pi$ is the scalar regret contrast
\[
  \theta_{\pi,\pi_0}=\E_{\mathcal L}\{Y(\pi_0)-Y(\pi)\},
\]
where $\mathcal L$ is a latent full-data law compatible with the observed law $P$ and
with the chosen local sensitivity restriction.  Positive values mean that $\pi$ has
larger loss, or lower welfare when $Y$ is welfare, than the status quo $\pi_0$ under
the sign convention inherited from the locked proposal.  The object in Section 7 is
not a point-identifying formula for $\theta_{\pi,\pi_0}$; it is a pair of observable
endpoints that brackets all locally compatible values of this target.  For a finite
active class $\Pi_\ast$, robust policy choice compares the upper endpoints
$R_A(P,\pi;t_A)$ across $\pi\in\Pi_\ast$.

\section{Main theorems}
\begin{theorem}[Sharp local support-function bounds]\label{thm:support-bounds}
Under Assumptions \ref{ass:bounded-overlap}--\ref{ass:support-budgets}, for every
$\pi\in\Pi$,
\[
  \Theta_C(P;\pi,\kappa)
  =
  [\,\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)-\kappa\|W_\pi\|_2,\,
     \theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+\kappa\|W_\pi\|_2\,],
\]
\[
  \Theta_M(P;\pi,\Gamma)
  =
  [\,\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)-b(\Gamma)D_1(W_\pi),\,
     \theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+b(\Gamma)D_1(W_\pi)\,],
\]
and
\[
  \Theta_S(P;\pi,\delta)
  =
  [\,\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)-\delta\|M_\pi\|_2,\,
     \theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)+\delta\|M_\pi\|_2\,].
\]
Thus $\Theta_I(P)\subseteq[\theta^A_L(P,\pi;t_A),\theta^A_U(P,\pi;t_A)]$ for
each language, and in the stated local linear support model the containment is sharp.
\end{theorem}

The novelty of Theorem \ref{thm:support-bounds} is the common policy-score display:
CH, MSM, and covariate-shift sensitivity become three different support norms of the
same $W_\pi$.  This is not a new estimator or a global hidden-confounding model; it is
the exact local partial-identification reduction needed before the finite calibration
question can be asked.

\begin{theorem}[Positive scalar-calibration frontier]\label{thm:calibration-frontier}
Suppose Assumptions \ref{ass:bounded-overlap}--\ref{ass:active-domain} hold.  A
non-degenerate three-language scalar bridge among $B_C$, $B_M$, and $B_S$ on
$\Pi_\ast$ exists on an interval of positive sensitivity values if and only if
$Q_{\Pi_\ast}$ lies on the positive common-scale frontier, equivalently
$\tau_{\Pi_\ast}=0$:
\[
  \Delta_{AB}(\pi,\pi')=0
  \quad\text{for all }A,B\in\{C,M,S\}\text{ and all }\pi,\pi'\in\Pi_\ast,
\]
with $n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi)>0$ for every $\pi\in\Pi_\ast$.  Equivalently,
there are positive constants $\alpha_C,\alpha_M,\alpha_S$ and positive policy weights
$\rho(\pi)$ such that
\[
  (n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi))
  =\rho(\pi)(\alpha_C,\alpha_M,\alpha_S)
  \quad\text{for every }\pi\in\Pi_\ast.
\]
Assumption \ref{ass:rank-one} is a sufficient primitive condition for this frontier.
If Assumption \ref{ass:two-score} holds, then no scalar maps
$\kappa\mapsto\Gamma(\kappa)$ and $\kappa\mapsto\delta(\kappa)$ can make
$B_C(P,\pi;\kappa)=B_M(P,\pi;\Gamma(\kappa))=B_S(P,\pi;\delta(\kappa))$ for all
$\pi\in\Pi_\ast$ on any non-degenerate interval with $\kappa>0$.
\end{theorem}

This theorem confirms the main mathematical content of Conjecture 1.  Its practical
importance is that scalar translation is governed by a finite matrix diagnostic, not by
the name of the sensitivity analysis.  It differs from prior CH, MSM, and external
validity results because those works define useful one-language robustness criteria,
whereas Theorem \ref{thm:calibration-frontier} characterizes when the criteria can be
made ranking-equivalent across a finite policy class.

\begin{proposition}[Reference-calibration ranking witness]\label{prop:ranking-witness}
Let $A,B\in\{C,M,S\}$ and let $\pi_1,\pi_2\in\Pi_\ast$ have positive $A$ and $B$
penalties.  Fix a positive scale $s_A$ for language $A$ and set the language-$B$
scale by reference-policy calibration,
\[
  s_B=s_A\,{n_A(\pi_1)\over n_B(\pi_1)}.
\]
If $\Delta_{AB}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0$, then the two penalty gaps
\[
  G_A=s_A\{n_A(\pi_1)-n_A(\pi_2)\},\qquad
  G_B=s_B\{n_B(\pi_1)-n_B(\pi_2)\}
\]
are distinct.  For every nominal regret difference
\[
  d=\theta^0_{\pi_1,\pi_0}-\theta^0_{\pi_2,\pi_0}
  \quad\text{strictly between }-G_A\text{ and }-G_B,
\]
the two robust-regret criteria
$\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}+s_A n_A(\pi)$ and
$\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}+s_B n_B(\pi)$ choose different unique minimizers among
$\{\pi_1,\pi_2\}$.
\end{proposition}

This proposition supplies the ranking part of Conjecture 1 under an explicit
calibration convention.  It is the finite-policy reason why a CH-calibrated MSM or
covariate-shift report can change a policy decision even when the reference policy's
penalty has been matched exactly.

\begin{proposition}[Nearest correct witness for Conjecture 2]\label{prop:conj2-witness}
Conjecture 2 is false as an open-set statement without an additional normalization
forcing $\|W_{\pi_1}\|_2=\|W_{\pi_2}\|_2$.  A point witness is available on four
equally likely atoms:
\[
  W_{\pi_1}=(-1,-1,1,1),\qquad
  W_{\pi_2}=(-\sqrt 3,1/\sqrt 3,1/\sqrt 3,1/\sqrt 3).
\]
Both scores are centered and have $L^2$ norm one, but
\[
  D_1(W_{\pi_1})=1,\qquad D_1(W_{\pi_2})=1/\sqrt 3.
\]
Hence CH ties the two penalties at this law while MSM separates them.  Moreover,
$\Delta_{CM}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0$ at the witness, so by continuity there is an open
neighborhood of laws and score values on which the non-calibration residual remains
nonzero.
\end{proposition}

The proposition refutes only the word ``open'' attached to exact equality of CH norms.
It preserves the intended economic warning: equal variance can hide distributional
shape differences that marginal-sensitivity penalties detect.

\section{Interpretation}
Theorem \ref{thm:support-bounds} says that the three sensitivity languages tighten or
widen policy-regret bounds through different observable features of the same local
score.  CH partial-$R^2$ sensitivity sees only the variance of $W_\pi$.  MSM sensitivity
sees the centered absolute-deviation geometry of $W_\pi$, so two policies with the
same variance can have different MSM penalties.  Covariate-shift sensitivity sees only
the covariate projection $M_\pi(X)$, so score variation orthogonal to $X$ does not
affect the covariate-shift bound.

The bound collapses to point identification when the relevant radius is zero
($\kappa=0$, $\Gamma=1$, or $\delta=0$) or when the relevant score norm is zero for
the policy being compared.  It widens as the sensitivity radius grows; if the local
model is abandoned and only the bounded-outcome restriction $Y\in[\ell,u]$ remains,
the natural no-restriction Manski-style regret bound is
$[\ell-u,u-\ell]$ for $Y(\pi_0)-Y(\pi)$.  Thus the tightening work in this note is
done entirely by the local CH, MSM, or covariate-shift support set, not by a shape
restriction such as monotone treatment response.

Theorem \ref{thm:calibration-frontier} gives the interpretation of scalar calibration.
A single policy can always be calibrated by choosing the other language's radius to
match that one policy's penalty.  Multiple policies require a stronger condition:
all active rows of $Q_{\Pi_\ast}$ must point in the same positive direction.  When the
condition fails, the scalar value reported in one sensitivity language cannot be used
as a policy-invariant substitute for another language's robustness curve.

\section{Step-by-step proof}
\begin{lemma}[Three support functions]\label{lem:support-functions}
Let $W\in L^2_0(P)\cap L^\infty(P)$ and $M(X)=\E[W\mid X]$.  Then
\[
  \sup_{\E h=0,\ \E h^2\le\kappa^2}\E[hW]=\kappa\|W\|_2,
\]
\[
  \sup_{\E h=0,\ \|h\|_\infty\le b}\E[hW]=b\inf_{c\in\R}\E|W-c|,
\]
and
\[
  \sup_{\E g(X)=0,\ \E g(X)^2\le\delta^2}\E[g(X)W]=\delta\|M\|_2.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For the CH ellipsoid, Cauchy--Schwarz gives
$\E[hW]\le \|h\|_2\|W\|_2\le\kappa\|W\|_2$.  If $\|W\|_2>0$, equality is attained by
$h=\kappa W/\|W\|_2$, which is mean zero because $\E W=0$; if $W=0$, both sides are
zero.

For the MSM bounded set, the mean-zero constraint on $h$ implies
$\E[hW]=\E[h(W-c)]$ for every constant $c$.  Hence
\[
  \E[hW]\le b\,\E|W-c|
  \quad\text{for every }c,
\]
and taking the infimum over $c$ gives the upper bound
$b\inf_c\E|W-c|$.  Conversely, choose a median $c_\ast$ of $W$.  The subgradient of
$c\mapsto\E|W-c|$ at a median contains zero, so one can choose a measurable
$s\in[-1,1]$ with $s=\operatorname{sign}(W-c_\ast)$ off the event
$\{W=c_\ast\}$ and $\E s=0$.  Then $h=bs$ is feasible and
$\E[hW]=b\E[s(W-c_\ast)]=b\E|W-c_\ast|$, proving equality.

For the covariate-shift ellipsoid, the tower property gives
\[
  \E[g(X)W]=\E[g(X)\E(W\mid X)]=\E[g(X)M(X)].
\]
Since $\E M=0$, Cauchy--Schwarz gives the upper bound
$\delta\|M\|_2$, attained by $g=\delta M/\|M\|_2$ when $M\ne0$ and equal to zero
when $M=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:support-bounds}]
Assumptions \ref{ass:finite-local} and \ref{ass:support-budgets} define each local
identified set as the nominal value plus all linear perturbations generated by the
corresponding support set.  Lemma \ref{lem:support-functions} gives the largest upward
perturbation for each language.  The support sets are symmetric: if a perturbation
$h$ or $g$ is feasible, then its negative is feasible.  Therefore the largest downward
perturbation is the negative of the same support value.  Adding these upward and
downward extrema to $\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)$ gives the three displayed intervals.
The equality of the identified set with the interval follows because each support set
is convex, symmetric, and contains $0$, so every value between the two extrema is
obtained by scaling an extremizing, or approximating extremizing, direction.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem \ref{thm:calibration-frontier}]
Write the three penalties as
\[
  B_C=s_C n_C,\qquad B_M=s_M n_M,\qquad B_S=s_S n_S,
\]
where $s_C=\kappa$, $s_M=b(\Gamma)$, and $s_S=\delta$.  By Assumption
\ref{ass:active-domain}, all active $n_A(\pi)$ are positive.  If a three-language
scalar bridge exists at a positive sensitivity value, then for every pair of languages
$A,B$ and every $\pi,\pi'\in\Pi_\ast$,
\[
  s_A n_A(\pi)=s_B n_B(\pi),
  \qquad
  s_A n_A(\pi')=s_B n_B(\pi').
\]
Eliminating the positive scales gives
\[
  n_A(\pi)n_B(\pi')-n_A(\pi')n_B(\pi)=0,
\]
so all minors vanish.  Since the bridge exists on an interval, the same argument holds
throughout the interval; no endpoint case with a zero scale is being used.

Conversely, suppose all minors vanish and all entries are positive.  Pick any reference
policy $\pi_0^\ast\in\Pi_\ast$ and define
\[
  \rho(\pi)=n_C(\pi),\quad
  \alpha_C=1,\quad
  \alpha_M={n_M(\pi_0^\ast)\over n_C(\pi_0^\ast)},\quad
  \alpha_S={n_S(\pi_0^\ast)\over n_C(\pi_0^\ast)}.
\]
The vanished minors imply $n_M(\pi)=\alpha_M\rho(\pi)$ and
$n_S(\pi)=\alpha_S\rho(\pi)$ for all active policies.  Given any $\kappa>0$, choose
$\Gamma$ and $\delta$ so that
\[
  b(\Gamma)=\kappa/\alpha_M,\qquad
  \delta=\kappa/\alpha_S.
\]
Assumption \ref{ass:support-budgets} guarantees that such a $\Gamma$ exists because
$b$ is continuous, strictly increasing, and has range $[0,\infty)$.  Then all three
penalties equal $\kappa\rho(\pi)$ for every $\pi\in\Pi_\ast$.

If Assumption \ref{ass:rank-one} holds, then
$n_C(\pi)=|c_\pi|\|W_0\|_2$ and
$n_M(\pi)=|c_\pi|D_1(W_0)$ by homogeneity of the $L^2$ norm and $D_1$.  The additional
condition $|d_\pi|$ proportional to $|c_\pi|$ gives
$n_S(\pi)=|d_\pi|\|M_0\|_2$, hence the three columns have a positive common scale.
If Assumption \ref{ass:two-score} holds, at least one minor is nonzero, contradicting
the necessary condition just proved; equivalently $\tau_{\Pi_\ast}>0$.  Thus no
non-degenerate scalar bridge exists.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:ranking-witness}]
The reference calibration makes the two language penalties equal at $\pi_1$:
$s_A n_A(\pi_1)=s_B n_B(\pi_1)$.  The difference between the two penalty gaps is
\[
  G_A-G_B
  =
  s_A\{n_A(\pi_1)-n_A(\pi_2)\}
  -s_A{n_A(\pi_1)\over n_B(\pi_1)}
   \{n_B(\pi_1)-n_B(\pi_2)\}.
\]
After canceling the equal $\pi_1$ terms,
\[
  G_A-G_B
  =
  {s_A\over n_B(\pi_1)}
  \{n_A(\pi_1)n_B(\pi_2)-n_A(\pi_2)n_B(\pi_1)\}
  =
  {s_A\over n_B(\pi_1)}\Delta_{AB}(\pi_1,\pi_2),
\]
which is nonzero.  For language $A$, policy $\pi_1$ has smaller robust regret than
$\pi_2$ exactly when $d+G_A<0$.  For language $B$, policy $\pi_1$ has smaller robust
regret exactly when $d+G_B<0$.  If $d$ lies strictly between $-G_A$ and $-G_B$, these
two strict inequalities have opposite truth values, so the unique minimizer among the
two policies differs.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition \ref{prop:conj2-witness}]
For the displayed scores under the uniform law on four atoms,
\[
  \E W_{\pi_1}=0,\qquad
  \E W_{\pi_2}={-\sqrt3+3/\sqrt3\over 4}=0.
\]
Their second moments are
\[
  \E W_{\pi_1}^2=1,\qquad
  \E W_{\pi_2}^2={3+3(1/3)\over4}=1.
\]
Thus the CH penalties are equal at every $\kappa$.  A median of $W_{\pi_1}$ can be any
point in $[-1,1]$, and the minimum absolute deviation is $1$.  A median of
$W_{\pi_2}$ is $1/\sqrt3$, since that value has probability $3/4$, and therefore
\[
  D_1(W_{\pi_2})
  ={1\over4}\left(\sqrt3+{1\over\sqrt3}\right)
  ={1\over\sqrt3}.
\]
So $\Delta_{CM}(\pi_1,\pi_2)=1\cdot(1/\sqrt3)-1\cdot1\ne0$.

The maps from a finite-support probability vector and finite score values to
$\|W_{\pi_j}\|_2$ and $D_1(W_{\pi_j})$ are continuous at this non-degenerate witness
as long as the median identity is not changed by a tie-breaking discontinuity; small
perturbations preserving the mass ordering do not change the median cell.  Hence the
strict residual inequality persists on an open neighborhood.  Exact equality of the
two $L^2$ norms, however, is the zero set of a nontrivial continuous scalar function.
For fixed finite-support score functions, if the equality of variances held on an open
probability neighborhood, the two variance polynomials would be identical; equivalently,
the pairwise squared distances of the two score arrays would agree, so in one dimension
the centered arrays differ only by a translation and a global sign, which also preserves
$D_1$.  Thus an open equality-and-separation witness requires an extra normalization
not present in the locked setup.  This proves the refutation and the nearest correct
open residual statement.
\end{proof}

\section{Sanity checks}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Zero sensitivity.}  If $\kappa=0$, $\Gamma=1$, and $\delta=0$, then
$B_C=B_M=B_S=0$ and every local identified set collapses to the nominal regret
$\theta^0_{\pi,\pi_0}(P)$.

\item \textbf{Single active policy.}  If $\Pi_\ast=\{\pi\}$ and all three penalties
are positive, scalar calibration is always possible for that one policy.  For example,
given $\kappa>0$, choose $b(\Gamma)=\kappa n_C(\pi)/n_M(\pi)$ and
$\delta=\kappa n_C(\pi)/n_S(\pi)$.  The frontier obstruction is therefore genuinely
multi-policy.

\item \textbf{Natural no-restriction bound.}  If the local support restrictions are
removed and only $Y\in[\ell,u]$ is maintained, then each potential outcome term lies
in $[\ell,u]$, so
$Y(\pi_0)-Y(\pi)\in[\ell-u,u-\ell]$ and the natural Manski-style regret bound is
$[\ell-u,u-\ell]$.  The local bounds in Theorem \ref{thm:support-bounds} are tighter
only because the CH, MSM, or covariate-shift support set is imposed.

\item \textbf{Zero-width nonzero-radius corner.}  If $W_\pi=0$ almost surely, then
$M_\pi=0$ and $D_1(W_\pi)=0$.  The policy is locally insensitive to all three
perturbation languages, so the identified interval is a point for every radius.

\item \textbf{Monotone-treatment-response specialization.}  No MTR restriction is
part of the locked parameter tuple.  If an MTR restriction were added in a separate
model, it would intersect the support-function interval with a one-sided causal
shape constraint; that is a different identified set and is not used in the
calibration theorem.

\item \textbf{Finite LP analogy.}  In a finite latent-response model such as the
Balke--Pearl binary-IV setting, sharp bounds are obtained by optimizing a linear
functional over a finite polytope.  The present theorem is the local convex-analytic
analog: the feasible polytope is replaced by one of the three explicit norm balls,
and the resulting support function has the closed forms in Section 7.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Counterexample or minimality check}
The four-atom construction in Proposition \ref{prop:conj2-witness} is the minimality
check for variance-only calibration.  It shows that CH's $L^2$ norm does not determine
MSM's centered absolute-deviation norm, even when both scores are centered, bounded,
and have equal variance.  Therefore a theorem that attempts to translate CH into MSM
using only $\|W_\pi\|_2$ is false for two policies.

The active-domain assumption is also necessary.  If $n_S(\pi)=0$ but
$n_C(\pi)>0$ for some active policy, then no positive scalar can make the CH and
covariate-shift penalties equal for that policy.  If the policy is retained anyway,
the all-pairs minor condition can be obscured by zero ratios; excluding zero-column
degeneracies is what makes the frontier a genuine positive calibration statement.

The policy-invariance of the scalar bridge is the other minimal condition.  If one
allows a different map $\Gamma_\pi(\kappa)$ or $\delta_\pi(\kappa)$ for every policy,
then calibration is trivial whenever the denominator penalty is positive:
choose the policy-specific radius to match that policy's penalty.  Such a map does
not preserve a common sensitivity scale and cannot justify comparing robust rankings
across policies.

The nearest formula that fixes Conjecture 2 is to replace the open equality claim by
an open residual claim:
\[
  \Delta_{CM}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  \Delta_{CS}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  \Delta_{MS}(\pi_1,\pi_2)\ne0.
\]
This condition is open under finite-support perturbations and is exactly the obstruction
used by Theorem \ref{thm:calibration-frontier} and Proposition \ref{prop:ranking-witness}.

\section{Formalization checklist}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Observable law.}  A probability law $P$ for $O=(X,A,Y)$ with
$A\in\{0,1\}$, $Y\in[\ell,u]$, and nominal propensity bounded away from $0$ and $1$.
Role: supplies the expectation, conditional expectation, and support conditions.

\item \textbf{Policy class.}  A finite set $\Pi$ of measurable maps
$\mathcal X\to\{0,1\}$ with status quo $\pi_0$ and active subset $\Pi_\ast$.
Role: makes the calibration diagnostic a finite matrix problem.

\item \textbf{Policy score map.}  For each $\pi$, a bounded centered score
$W_\pi\in L^2_0(P)\cap L^\infty(P)$ and projection $M_\pi(X)=\E[W_\pi\mid X]$.
Role: represents the first-order perturbation of the latent regret target.

\item \textbf{Probabilistic assumptions.}  Bounded outcome and overlap; existence and
boundedness of the derivative scores; existence of conditional expectations; positivity
of active penalties for non-degenerate bridges.

\item \textbf{Sensitivity sets.}  $\mathcal H_C(\kappa)$ as a mean-zero $L^2$ ball,
$\mathcal H_M(\Gamma)$ as a mean-zero $L^\infty$ ball with radius $b(\Gamma)$, and
$\mathcal G_S(\delta)$ as a covariate-only mean-zero $L^2$ ball.  Role: define the
local identified sets and their support functions.

\item \textbf{Deterministic support identities.}  Cauchy--Schwarz extremizer for
$L^2$ balls; dual identity
$\sup_{\E h=0,\|h\|_\infty\le b}\E[hW]=b\inf_c\E|W-c|$; tower-property reduction
$\E[g(X)W]=\E[g(X)M(X)]$.

\item \textbf{Median identity.}  For integrable $W$, the minimizers of
$c\mapsto\E|W-c|$ are medians of $W$.  Role: makes $D_1(W)$ an evaluable
one-dimensional functional and supplies the MSM support-function extremizer.

\item \textbf{Sharp interval identity.}  A symmetric convex support set produces the
interval from the negative support value to the positive support value.  Role: converts
support functions into sharp local partial-identification bounds.

\item \textbf{Calibration matrix algebra.}  Positive vectors
$(n_C(\pi),n_M(\pi),n_S(\pi))_{\pi\in\Pi_\ast}$; all-pairs residuals
$\Delta_{AB}(\pi,\pi')$; equivalence between vanished $2\times2$ minors and positive
rank-one column structure.  Role: proves the necessary and sufficient bridge theorem.

\item \textbf{Ranking witness algebra.}  Given two policies and two languages, reference
calibration at one policy makes the gap difference proportional to
$\Delta_{AB}(\pi_1,\pi_2)$.  Role: turns non-calibration into an explicit open interval
of nominal regret differences with different unique minimizers.

\item \textbf{Finite-support witness.}  Four-atom uniform law with scores
$(-1,-1,1,1)$ and $(-\sqrt3,1/\sqrt3,1/\sqrt3,1/\sqrt3)$.  Role: separates equal
$L^2$ penalties from centered absolute-deviation penalties and refutes Conjecture 2's
open equality wording.

\item \textbf{Substrate survey record, bypass-justified.}  The Manski-style bounded
potential-outcome interval primitives fit the no-restriction sanity check but not the
local policy-score support theorem.  The MTR/MTS/MIV and combined shape-restriction
families are bypassed because the locked restrictions are local CH, MSM, and
covariate-shift balls, not monotonicity restrictions.  The Balke--Pearl, Lee, proxy,
and general partial-ID catalogues are bypassed because they encode finite IV,
selection, proxy, or generic interval problems at a different abstraction.  The
mathematical plan therefore uses expectation, conditional projection, norm, median,
support-function, and finite-matrix objects directly.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion and references}
The theorem-level contribution is a sharp local bound and calibration frontier for
policy-regret sensitivity.  CH, MSM, and covariate-shift ambiguity tighten the natural
bounded-outcome regret range by restricting perturbations to, respectively, a mean-zero
$L^2$ ellipsoid, a mean-zero bounded perturbation set, and a covariate-only $L^2$
tilt.  The bound becomes point-identifying at zero radius or for a policy score that is
invisible to the relevant perturbation set; it becomes vacuous, relative to this local
analysis, when those sensitivity restrictions are removed and only bounded outcomes
remain.  A scalar calibration across languages is valid over a finite policy class
exactly on the positive common-scale frontier of the diagnostic matrix $Q_{\Pi_\ast}$.
Off that frontier, a single reported sensitivity value cannot generally be translated
into another language without changing robust policy-regret rankings.
